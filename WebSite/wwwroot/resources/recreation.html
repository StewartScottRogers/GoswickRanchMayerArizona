Recreation & Lifestyle

What to do, see, and experience within the Bradshaw Mountains foothills and the surrounding 60-mile radius.

Dark Skies — Bortle Class 3

Goswick Ranch sits under Bortle Class 3 skies — a level of darkness rarely available within two hours of a major metropolitan area. On clear nights, the Milky Way is a brilliant structural band with distinct lanes and clouds of nebulosity visible to the naked eye. The Gegenschein (a faint oval glow opposite the Sun) and the entire zodiacal band are routinely visible. M33 (the Triangulum Galaxy) can be seen without optical aid under good conditions.

Peak viewing season runs from October through June. The monsoon season (July–September) brings cloud cover and atmospheric moisture that reduces transparency. October and November offer some of the best conditions: dark skies, dry air after the monsoons, and moderate temperatures that make extended observing sessions comfortable.

The nearest significant light pollution source is Prescott, 27 miles to the west — its glow is largely blocked by the Bradshaw ridgeline. Phoenix is 75 miles south; its dome is visible on the southern horizon but does not significantly impact viewing overhead.

Wildlife

The Madrean chaparral and lower ponderosa zone surrounding Goswick Ranch supports a diverse and sometimes surprising mix of wildlife. What to expect by season:

Mammals

  • Mule deer — The deer of the Bradshaw Mountains are mule deer (not white-tailed deer). Commonly seen at dawn and dusk, particularly along creek drainages and woodland edges. Bucks are at their most visible in autumn during the rut (November–December).
  • Mountain lions — Present and active throughout the Bradshaws. Mountain lions are elusive and rarely encountered, but they are real predators in this landscape — the same terrain where Giles Wesley Goswick worked as a government lion hunter in the early 20th century. Protect small pets and livestock; do not leave them unattended outdoors at night.
  • Wild turkeys — Common at middle elevations, particularly near water. Rio Grande turkeys are year-round residents of the Prescott National Forest.
  • Coatimundi — Occasional visitors from the south during summer; travel in social groups and are unmistakable with their long ringed tails.
  • Black bears — Uncommon but present in the upper Bradshaws, especially during late summer when piñon nuts and juniper berries ripen. Secure trash and outdoor food storage.
  • Javelina (collared peccary) — Common at lower elevations; may be seen at dawn or dusk near cactus patches.
  • Coyote, bobcat, gray fox — All present and frequently heard if not seen. Coyotes are vocal year-round.

Reptiles

  • Black-tailed rattlesnake and Arizona black rattlesnake — Both species have been documented at elevations up to 6,000 feet in the Bradshaws. Watch where you step and reach, especially from April through October. These are venomous snakes; give them space and they will move on.
  • Gila monster — Rare at elevations above 5,000 feet, but possible. One of only two venomous lizard species in the U.S.; slow-moving and unlikely to be encountered, but legally protected — do not disturb.
  • Bull snake, king snake, gopher snake — Common non-venomous species; beneficial predators of rodents.

Birds

The transition zone between chaparral and ponderosa pine is a birding hotspot. Year-round residents include zone-tailed hawks, Mexican jays, acorn woodpeckers, Steller's jays, white-breasted nuthatches, and canyon towhees. Summer brings western tanagers, Scott's orioles, and multiple hummingbird species. Migrant species pass through in April–May and September–October.

Hiking & Outdoor Recreation

Prescott National Forest encompasses the Bradshaw Mountains and offers more than 450 miles of trails for hiking, mountain biking, and horseback riding. The forest is largely accessible without permits for day use. Trail conditions and road closures are available at the Prescott National Forest website.

Seasonal Highlights

  • Spring (April–May): Wildflower season — penstemon, lupine, globe mallow, owl's clover, and brittlebush in bloom. The Bradshaws are at their most colorful and comfortable.
  • Summer (June–Sept): Monsoon season. Morning hikes before afternoon storms build; dramatic cloud formations and lightning displays in the afternoons. Wildfire risk is high pre-monsoon (May–June).
  • Fall (October–November): The Senator Highway corridor (Forest Road 52) through the upper Bradshaws offers spectacular fall color: bigtooth maple, Gambel oak, sumac, and cottonwood turn gold, orange, and red. Peak color typically arrives in mid-to-late October. This drive is one of central Arizona's most underrated autumn experiences.
  • Winter (December–March): Snow at upper elevations (Mt. Union 7,979 ft). Forest roads may require 4WD or chains. Snowshoeing and cross-country skiing possible in high-snow years. Star-gazing season (dry, clear air).

Equestrian Recreation

Goswick Ranch's 9-acre lots and direct access to Prescott National Forest make it exceptionally well-suited for horse ownership. Horses can be trailered to forest trailheads within 15–30 minutes of the community.

  • Groom Creek Horse Camp — Prescott National Forest; one of Arizona's most popular equestrian campgrounds; corrals, water, and forest trails
  • Lynx Lake Recreation Area — 5 miles east of Prescott; equestrian-friendly trails around the lake; note that the boat launch ramp was closed in late 2024 for valve repairs (check status before visiting)
  • Hundreds of miles of forest trails — Many accessible directly from trailheads within 20 miles of Goswick Ranch

For equestrian services and supply, see Local Services for information on Los Caballos mobile equine veterinary services, Olsen's Grain, and Prescott Livestock Auction.

Agua Fria National Monument

The Agua Fria National Monument lies approximately 20–30 miles south of Goswick Ranch — managed by the Bureau of Land Management (BLM) and one of Arizona's most overlooked public lands. The monument protects a dramatic basalt mesa landscape cut by the Agua Fria River canyon, with:

  • Well-preserved prehistoric pueblo sites and thousands of petroglyphs (rock carvings)
  • Dispersed primitive camping — free, no permits required for most areas
  • Excellent birding (golden eagles, prairie falcons, zone-tailed hawks)
  • No entrance fees; managed by the BLM Hassayampa Field Office

Access is primarily via I-17 to the Bloody Basin Road exit (Exit 259), then east on Bloody Basin Road — the same road where Mark Goswick Cattle Co. operates.

Arcosanti

Arcosanti is a unique experimental urban community designed by Italian architect Paolo Soleri, located approximately 11–15 miles from Goswick Ranch via I-17 and Cordes Junction. Soleri spent decades constructing this visionary city-in-miniature as a living laboratory for "arcology" — the combination of architecture and ecology. Guided tours are available Thursday through Monday. The community is also the source of the famous Cosanti wind bells — bronze and ceramic bells cast on-site and sold worldwide.

Jerome National Historic Landmark

Jerome, Arizona is approximately 40 miles northeast of Goswick Ranch (~50 minutes via AZ-69 and US-89A). Once called the "Wickedest Town in the West" during its copper mining heyday, Jerome is now a vibrant arts and tourism destination perched on the side of Mingus Mountain at 5,200 feet.

  • Jerome Grand Hotel — a 1927 former hospital converted to a hotel; dramatic views and a storied history
  • Caduceus Cellars — winery owned by Maynard James Keenan (frontman of the rock band Tool); located in Jerome and part of the Arizona wine resurgence
  • Galleries, restaurants, and historic mine tours

Verde Valley Wine Trail

The Verde Valley Wine Trail was designated an American Viticultural Area (AVA) in 2021, partly through the efforts of Maynard James Keenan, who owns Caduceus Cellars. Approximately 15 tasting rooms are now open along the trail — an increasingly serious wine destination. Jerome and Cottonwood are the primary hubs, reachable from Goswick Ranch in about 45–60 minutes via AZ-69 and AZ-89A (route through Prescott / Chino Valley is scenic).

Key wineries on the trail:

  • Page Springs Cellars (Cornville) — One of Arizona's most acclaimed wineries; Rhône-style varietals; bistro, tasting room, and tours along Oak Creek
  • Alcantara Vineyards (Verde River/Oak Creek confluence, near Cottonwood) — One of the largest central Arizona vineyards; live music, events, hay rides, e-bike and kayaking tours
  • Caduceus Cellars / Merkin Vineyards (tasting room in Jerome; vineyards in Verde Valley and Willcox) — Founded by Maynard James Keenan; 110 acres under vine, 14,000 cases/year; Italian and Spanish varietals; drove the AVA designation effort
  • Javelina Leap Vineyard & Winery (Page Springs Road, Cornville) — Boutique winery known for red wines
  • Jerome Winery — In Jerome itself; historic hillside setting

Best time to visit: spring and fall for comfortable temperatures; harvest season (late summer / early fall) offers the most immersive experience.

Tuzigoot & Montezuma Castle

Two of Arizona's most significant prehistoric sites lie approximately 27–28 miles from Goswick Ranch:

  • Tuzigoot National Monument (Clarkdale) — Sinagua pueblo ruins on a hilltop overlooking the Verde River
  • Montezuma Castle National Monument (Camp Verde) — five-story cliff dwelling built into a limestone alcove around 1100 CE; one of the best-preserved cliff dwellings in North America

A single $10 entrance fee covers admission to both monuments for 7 consecutive days; a combined visit takes about half a day.

Prescott

Prescott (27 miles northwest, ~40 minutes) is the cultural, medical, and commercial hub for Goswick Ranch residents. Arizona's former territorial capital sits at 5,319 feet — mile-high mountain climate with a walkable Victorian-era downtown. Key highlights:

Whiskey Row & Downtown

Montezuma Street flanking Courthouse Plaza is home to Arizona's oldest saloons and restaurants. The block burned in 1900; legend holds that drinkers carried the bar out to the plaza to keep drinking during the fire. Today the mix includes:

  • The Palace Restaurant & Saloon — Arizona's oldest continuously operating saloon (rebuilt after the 1900 fire); known for steaks and Western memorabilia
  • Prescott Brewing Company — Gurley Street across from Courthouse Square; opened 1994; family-friendly brewpub; award-winning beers distributed statewide; one of Arizona's pioneering craft breweries
  • Matt's Saloon — Country dancing and live bands; classic Prescott nightlife

Sharlot Hall Museum

One of Arizona's premier history museums, on nearly four acres of grounds with 11 exhibit buildings (6 are original historic structures). Includes the Governor's Mansion (1864), Fort Misery (Arizona's oldest log cabin, moved here in 1934), and the Frémont House (1875).
Hours: May–September Mon–Sat 10am–5pm, Sun noon–4pm; Oct–April Mon–Sat 10am–4pm, Sun noon–4pm
Admission: Adults $9 | Seniors $8 | Active military $6 | Youth 13–17 $5 | Children under 12 free

Phippen Museum of Western Art

Located at 4701 N Highway 89 north of downtown. Dedicated to the art of the American West; named for cowboy artist George Phippen (co-founder of the Cowboy Artists of America). Features the Arizona Rancher & Cowboy Hall of Fame with a working chuck wagon.
Hours: Tue–Sat 10am–4pm, Sun 1–4pm · Admission: Adults $12 | Students $5 | Under 12 free

Prescott Events

  • World's Oldest Rodeo — Runs continuously since 1888; held annually late June through July 4th week (June 30–July 6, 2025 was the 138th annual); 27,000+ attendees; sells out all performances. The centerpiece of Prescott's Fourth of July celebration.
  • Summer Concert Series — Free live music at Courthouse Plaza most Tuesday, Thursday, and Friday evenings, June through September
  • 4th Friday Art Walk — 16 galleries open the 4th Friday of each month starting at 5pm; since 2004
  • Prescott Film Festival — Annual July festival at Yavapai College and the historic Elks Theater
  • Prescott Farmers Market — Saturdays at 900 Iron Springs Rd; Nov–March 9:30am–12:30pm; April–October 7:30am–noon

Prescott Regional Airport

Ernest A. Love Field (PRC) is approximately 27–28 miles from Goswick Ranch — the same distance as Prescott city center via SR 69. For many trips, it is dramatically more convenient than Phoenix Sky Harbor.

  • United Express (SkyWest) operates daily year-round service to Los Angeles (LAX) and Denver (DEN) — with one connection, you can reach hundreds of destinations via United's hubs
  • Parking: Free long-term parking (no reservations; no fees; max 10 days)
  • Car rental: Avis, Budget, Enterprise, Hertz, and Eagle Rent-A-Car at baggage claim
  • For destinations not served via United, Sky Harbor (PHX) is approximately 74 miles / ~70 minutes via SR 69 and I-17

Sedona

Sedona lies approximately 60 miles northeast of Goswick Ranch (~1 hour via AZ-69 and AZ-89A). The red rock scenery, canyon hikes, and vortex culture make it one of Arizona's most visited destinations. For the best experience, arrive before 7 AM on weekends — trailhead parking lots fill by mid-morning. Oak Creek Canyon (the scenic drive between Sedona and Flagstaff) is spectacular in late October when the cottonwoods and maples turn.
