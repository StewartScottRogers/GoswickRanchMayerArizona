Mayer Fire District

The Mayer Fire District serves Goswick Ranch for fire, medical, rescue, and advanced life support (ALS) emergencies. It is a combination district (paid and volunteer) with three stations (Stations 21, 22, and 23). The primary service area covers approximately 8,000 residents across 52 square miles; extended response coverage reaches an additional 2,000 people across roughly 302 square miles. The district also provides first-responder coverage to Prescott National Forest, BLM lands, state lands, and Agua Fria National Monument.

Response times vary based on terrain and road conditions — residents are encouraged to clearly mark their address at the property entrance to assist emergency responders.

Fire District address: 11975 S State Route 69, Spring Valley, AZ 86333
Website: yavcofire.org — Mayer Fire District

Sheriff's Office

Yavapai County Sheriff's Office (YCSO) provides law enforcement for unincorporated Yavapai County, including Goswick Ranch. For non-emergency matters, call the main YCSO line at (928) 771-3260, or contact the Mayer Substation directly at (928) 771-3509. For emergencies, always call 911.

YCSO website: ycsoaz.gov
Email: web.sheriff@yavapaiaz.gov

Nearest Hospital & Medevac

Yavapai Regional Medical Center (YRMC) — Prescott Valley
Prescott Valley, AZ (approximately 13 miles from Mayer)
(928) 445-2700
~15–20 minutes from Goswick Ranch

YRMC is a Level IV Trauma Center — it can stabilize trauma patients, but complex trauma cases are transferred. For Level I trauma care, the nearest facility is in Phoenix (~73 miles, ~71 minutes from Mayer). Native Air 4 (helicopter based at YRMC West) received First Responder Status in December 2024 and provides medevac service to this area. In serious trauma emergencies, air transport to a Phoenix Level I Trauma Center should be requested when calling 911.

Wildfire Preparedness

The Bradshaw Mountains area has a well-documented wildfire history. Notable recent fires:

  • Brady Fire (February 2025) — approximately 400 acres near Pine Flats, about 10 miles from Goswick Ranch
  • Crooks Fire (April 2022) — 9,402 acres near Mount Union; 8 structures lost; threatened communities throughout the central Bradshaws
  • Goodwin Fire (June–July 2017) — 28,516 acres; triggered evacuations of Mayer and Breezy Pines on June 27, 2017

The Bradshaw range now has ALERTWildfire cameras mounted on Mt. Francis and Mt. Union — these high-definition pan-tilt cameras can detect fires under 1 acre. Residents can monitor camera feeds in real time at alertwildfire.org.

Defensible space is your most important protection. Arizona's defensible space standard calls for a Zone 1 (0–30 feet) cleared of dead/dry vegetation and a Zone 2 (30–100 feet) with reduced fuel continuity. Goswick Ranch's 9-acre minimum lot size means residents have substantial space to create buffer zones around structures.

The Firewise USA program, administered by the National Fire Protection Association, provides a framework for community-level wildfire preparedness. USAA insurance offers up to a 5% discount for homes in certified Firewise communities. Contact the HOA board if you're interested in pursuing Firewise certification for Goswick Ranch.

Evacuation Alerts — Sign Up Now

Yavapai County uses Genasys Protect for evacuation zone management (launched April 2024). Look up your evacuation zone at protect.genasys.com. Know your zone before a fire starts — evacuation orders are issued by zone, and roads can become impassable quickly in wildfire conditions.

Sign up for AlertYavapai / Smart911 emergency notifications:

  • Web: smart911.com
  • Text: Send alertYAVAPAI to 78015

Smart911 allows you to pre-register your address, household members, and special needs so that dispatchers have your information automatically when you call 911.

Public Safety Power Shutoffs (PSPS)

APS has identified the Mayer area as a zone where it may implement Public Safety Power Shutoffs during extreme wildfire weather conditions (high heat, low humidity, strong winds). These shutoffs can last hours or days. Residents on private wells will lose water pressure when power is cut because well pumps require electricity to operate.

Recommended preparedness:

  • Maintain a generator capable of running your well pump (minimum 3,000 watts for most residential well pumps)
  • Store at least 72 hours of drinking water (1 gallon per person per day minimum)
  • Sign up for APS outage notifications at aps.com

Homeowners Insurance

Arizona homeowners insurance rates increased 48% from 2021 to 2024 — the third-highest increase nationally — driven largely by wildfire risk in Yavapai and Maricopa counties. Rural Bradshaw Mountains properties typically fall in ISO Protection Class 9 or 10 (the highest-risk categories), which reflects their distance from fire stations and hydrants.

Key points for Goswick Ranch homeowners:

  • Arizona does not have a FAIR Plan (insurer of last resort), so residents who cannot find private coverage have limited options
  • USAA offers up to a 5% discount for Firewise USA–certified communities
  • Documented defensible space work, Class A roofing materials, and ember-resistant vents can improve insurability
  • Insurance companies may inspect properties and require vegetation clearance before issuing or renewing coverage — plan ahead

Flash Flood Preparedness

Big Bug Creek can rise from a trickle to a torrent in under an hour during monsoon storms (typically July–September). The creek's catchment basin is large and drains steep terrain — rainfall 10 miles away can send a flash flood down the canyon with little warning. Do not attempt to cross flooded roadways. "Turn Around, Don't Drown."

Big Bug Creek is designated FEMA Flood Zone AE (1% annual probability of flooding) in portions of its floodplain. Check with Yavapai County Planning for your specific flood zone designation if your parcel is near the creek.

Post-fire flooding is a particular risk: after the Goodwin Fire, hydrophobic soil in the burn scar caused catastrophic runoff that damaged or destroyed 109 homes along Big Bug Creek on July 19, 2017. After any significant wildfire in the watershed, be especially alert during the first monsoon season that follows.
