Mayer Fire District

The Mayer Fire District serves Goswick Ranch for fire, medical, rescue, and advanced life support (ALS) emergencies. It is a combination district (paid and volunteer) with three stations (Stations 21, 22, and 23). The primary service area covers approximately 8,000 residents across 52 square miles; extended response coverage reaches an additional 2,000 people across roughly 302 square miles. The district also provides first-responder coverage to Prescott National Forest, BLM lands, state lands, and Agua Fria National Monument.

Response times vary based on terrain and road conditions — residents are encouraged to clearly mark their address at the property entrance to assist emergency responders.

Fire District address: 11975 S State Route 69, Spring Valley, AZ 86333
Website: yavcofire.org — Mayer Fire District

Sheriff's Office

Yavapai County Sheriff's Office (YCSO) provides law enforcement for unincorporated Yavapai County, including Goswick Ranch. For non-emergency matters, call the main YCSO line at (928) 771-3260, or contact the Mayer Substation directly at (928) 771-3509. For emergencies, always call 911.

YRMC is a Level IV Trauma Center — it can stabilize trauma patients, but complex trauma cases are transferred. For Level I trauma care, the nearest facility is in Phoenix (~74 miles, ~70 minutes from Goswick Ranch). Native Air 4 (helicopter based at YRMC West) received First Responder Status in December 2024 and provides medevac service to this area. In serious trauma emergencies, air transport to a Phoenix Level I Trauma Center should be requested when calling 911.

Wildfire Preparedness

The Bradshaw Mountains area has a well-documented wildfire history. Notable recent fires:

  • Brady Fire (February 2025) — approximately 400 acres; triggered GO evacuations for zones YCU-2174 and YCU-2216 in the Big Bug/Poland Road corridor; roughly 10 miles from Goswick Ranch
  • Crooks Fire (April 2022) — 9,402 acres near Mount Union; 8 structures lost; threatened communities throughout the central Bradshaws
  • Goodwin Fire (June–July 2017) — 28,516 acres; triggered evacuations of Mayer and Breezy Pines on June 27, 2017; post-fire flooding on July 19, 2017 destroyed 109 homes along Big Bug Creek

The Bradshaw range has ALERTWildfire cameras mounted on Mt. Francis and Mt. Union — high-definition pan-tilt cameras capable of detecting fires under 1 acre. Monitor feeds in real time at alertwildfire.org.

The Mayer/Poland Road area has a Community Wildfire Protection Plan (CWPP), most recently updated in 2023. The CWPP identifies the Poland Road corridor — including the area around Goswick Ranch — as high-priority for fuel reduction treatments and defensible space education. Contact Mayer Fire District for current CWPP implementation status.

Defensible Space — Three Zones

Defensible space is your most important structural protection. Arizona's standard is three zones around every structure:

  • Zone 0 (0–5 feet): The ember-resistant zone. Non-combustible materials only — concrete, rock, gravel, brick. No wood mulch. No combustible plants, dead leaves, or stored materials immediately against the structure. Screen vents with 1/8-inch metal mesh to exclude embers. This is the most critical zone — ember intrusion through vents and gaps is the primary cause of structure ignition during wildfires.
  • Zone 1 (5–30 feet): The lean, clean, and green zone. Remove all dead/dry vegetation. Space plants so fire cannot climb from shrubs to trees (no "ladder fuels"). Keep grass irrigated and mowed to 4 inches or less. Prune trees up 6–10 feet from the ground. Remove branches overhanging the roof.
  • Zone 2 (30–100 feet): The reduced fuel zone. Reduce density and continuity of vegetation. Space shrubs and trees so that fire cannot spread continuously to Zone 1. Remove dead material. On slopes, extend Zone 2 further downhill — fire travels faster uphill.

Goswick Ranch's 9-acre minimum lot size means residents have substantial space to create effective buffer zones around structures. The Firewise USA program provides a community-level framework for wildfire preparedness. USAA insurance offers up to a 5% discount for homes in certified Firewise communities. Contact the HOA board to explore Firewise certification for Goswick Ranch.

Free Slash Drop-Off — Yavapai County Transfer Station

The Yavapai County Transfer Station (Mayer) at 11250 S Antelope Creek Rd — just minutes from Goswick Ranch — accepts free slash and brush disposal each spring to help residents create defensible space:

  • 2025 program dates: March 20 – June 15
  • Accepted: branches, limbs, brush, slash (cut vegetation)
  • Not accepted: roots, cacti, lumber, garbage, stumps, metal
  • No fee for slash/brush; transfer station phone: (928) 632-1427

Check current year dates through Yavapai County Public Works — dates typically run March through early June.

Evacuation Alerts — Sign Up Now

Yavapai County uses Genasys Protect for evacuation zone management (launched April 2024). Look up your evacuation zone at protect.genasys.com. Know your zone before a fire starts — evacuation orders are issued by zone, and roads can become impassable quickly in wildfire conditions.

Evacuation Route Vulnerability — Know This Now

SR-69 is the only paved evacuation corridor for Poland Road and the surrounding Bradshaw foothills. In a fast-moving fire, SR-69 can become congested or cut off. Know alternative unpaved routes (Poland Road → Five Corners → Forest Roads) in advance, and ensure your vehicle can handle them. Identify meeting points and out-of-area contacts before an emergency. During the Brady Fire (February 2025), evacuation orders affected zones on both sides of the Poland Road corridor simultaneously.

Sign up for AlertYavapai / Smart911 emergency notifications:

  • Web: smart911.com
  • Text: Send alertYAVAPAI to 78015

Smart911 allows you to pre-register your address, household members, and special needs so that dispatchers have your information automatically when you call 911.

Large Animal Evacuation — L.A.S.E.R.

Residents with horses, livestock, or other large animals should pre-register with L.A.S.E.R. (Large Animal Safe Emergency Response), the Yavapai County large animal evacuation program coordinated through the Yavapai County Sheriff's Office and local agricultural groups:

  • Pre-register animals, trailer availability, and assistance needs before an emergency
  • Identify neighbors with trailers who can help move animals on short notice
  • Keep trailers hitched or quickly accessible during high-fire-danger periods
  • Mark animals with livestock paint or temporary ID in case of separation
  • Evacuate animals early — do not wait for a mandatory evacuation order; once roads are congested, trailer movement becomes extremely difficult

Contact YCSO or Yavapai County Emergency Management for current L.A.S.E.R. registration information: yavapaiaz.gov/emergency

Public Safety Power Shutoffs (PSPS)

APS has identified the Mayer area as a zone where it may implement Public Safety Power Shutoffs during extreme wildfire weather conditions (high heat, low humidity, strong winds). These shutoffs can last hours or days. Residents on private wells will lose water pressure when power is cut because well pumps require electricity to operate.

Recommended preparedness:

  • Maintain a generator capable of running your well pump (minimum 3,000 watts for most residential well pumps)
  • Store at least 72 hours of drinking water (1 gallon per person per day minimum)
  • Sign up for APS outage notifications at aps.com
  • Keep propane tanks above 20% — PSPS events during high fire danger can coincide with evacuation orders

Flash Flood Preparedness

Big Bug Creek can rise from a trickle to a torrent in under an hour during monsoon storms (typically July–September). The creek's catchment basin is large and drains steep Bradshaw Mountain terrain — rainfall 10 miles away can send a flash flood down the canyon with little warning. Do not attempt to cross flooded roadways. "Turn Around, Don't Drown."

Big Bug Creek is designated FEMA Flood Zone AE (1% annual probability of flooding) in portions of its floodplain. Check with Yavapai County Planning for your specific flood zone designation if your parcel is near the creek.

Post-fire flooding is a particular risk: after the Goodwin Fire, hydrophobic soil in the burn scar caused catastrophic runoff that damaged or destroyed 109 homes along Big Bug Creek on July 19, 2017. After any significant wildfire in the watershed, be especially alert during the first monsoon season that follows.

Monitor Big Bug Creek gauge levels during monsoon season: USGS Big Bug Creek gauge (site 09507980)

Communications — HAM Radio

In a major emergency, cellular networks may be congested or offline. Licensed amateur (HAM) radio operators can communicate independently of cell and internet infrastructure:

  • Mt. Union Repeater — high-elevation repeater covering much of the central Bradshaws; useful for local coordination during emergencies
  • GMRS repeater — General Mobile Radio Service (no exam required; FRS/GMRS radios sold at hardware stores) provides local radio coverage for unlicensed users on designated channels
  • The Prescott Amateur Radio Club (prescottarc.net) is the regional HAM organization; licensing is a 35-question exam available at club sessions

Consider keeping at minimum a GMRS/FRS two-way radio (charged and accessible) for neighborhood coordination if cellular service fails during an emergency.

Homeowners Insurance

Arizona homeowners insurance rates increased 48% from 2021 to 2024 — the third-highest increase nationally — driven largely by wildfire risk in Yavapai and Maricopa counties. Rural Bradshaw Mountains properties typically fall in ISO Protection Class 9 or 10 (the highest-risk categories), which reflects their distance from fire stations and hydrants.

Key points for Goswick Ranch homeowners:

  • Arizona does not have a FAIR Plan (insurer of last resort), so residents who cannot find private coverage have limited options
  • USAA offers up to a 5% discount for Firewise USA–certified communities
  • Documented defensible space work, Class A roofing materials, and ember-resistant vents can improve insurability
  • Insurance companies may inspect properties and require vegetation clearance before issuing or renewing coverage — plan ahead
