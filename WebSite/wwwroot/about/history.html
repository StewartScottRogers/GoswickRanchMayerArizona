The History of Goswick Ranch

From a pioneering Arizona cattle family to one of the Bradshaw Mountains' most productive mining districts to a private mountain community.

The Goswick Family in Yavapai County

The Goswick name has been part of Yavapai County since the 1860s, when the American West was still being settled and the Bradshaw Mountains were known primarily for gold, silver, and cattle.

William Isaac Goswick (1836–1904) was among the earliest Goswicks in the region, working as a mail carrier between Camp Verde and Payson — an important Arizona Army post — during the frontier era (1884–1914).

Giles Wesley Goswick (1888–1975) left perhaps the most vivid mark. He worked as a government lion hunter in the Bradshaw foothills, tracking and removing mountain lions that threatened livestock — a respected and necessary role in ranching communities of the early twentieth century. Giles spent decades in the mountains that now surround the community bearing his family name.

George Allen Goswick (1919–1997) appears in the 1930 U.S. Census in Mayer, Yavapai County — placing the family firmly in this exact community for at least a century.

The family's cattle operation continued through the twentieth century and into the present day. Mark Goswick Cattle Co. operates today on Bloody Basin Road in the Mayer area (928-632-7730) — a living link to the ranching tradition that shaped this land. The Goswick Cattle Co. also held BLM mining claim "SPANISH #8," reflecting the combined ranching-and-mining identity typical of Bradshaw Mountain families.

From Ranch to Community

The original Goswick Ranch homestead, at 5905 S Goswick Ranch Rd, was built in 1948–1949. The 82-acre property served as the working ranch headquarters for the Goswick cattle operation for decades.

The modern Goswick Ranch subdivision — all 850.63 acres of it, recorded as Plat Book 56, Pages 8–12 in the Yavapai County Recorder's office — was carved from this historic ranch in Sections 31 and 32, T 13 N, R 1 E. The minimum 9-acre lots were set to preserve the open, ranch-scale character of the original land.

What was once a working cattle ranch became a private residential community. The name Goswick, earned through more than a century of family presence in these mountains, stayed with the land.

Poland Road — The Road Through History

Poland Road is the road residents travel every time they enter or leave Goswick Ranch. It is also one of the Bradshaw Mountains' most historically significant corridors.

The road is named for David Robert Poland (1834–1882), a prospector who arrived in Yavapai County around 1863. Poland worked Lynx Creek placers, co-founded the New Era mine in 1870, and in 1872 discovered the gold, silver, copper, and lead deposit that became the Poland Mine. In October 1879, he sold the mine to Chicago investors for approximately $3.5 million in modern dollars.

After Poland's death in 1882, developer Frank Murphy — also responsible for the Bradshaw Mountain Railroad — purchased and redeveloped the mine. In 1902, Murphy built a 20-stamp mill and concentrator on site. Ore was crushed in the stamp mill, mixed with water, piped down the mountain, loaded onto narrow-gauge ore cars, and hauled by mule through a tunnel to the Poland railroad junction — then shipped by rail to smelters in Humboldt and Mayer. In 1907 alone, the mill produced $130,465 in gold and 16,609 ounces of silver.

In ~1903, Murphy constructed the Poland-Walker Tunnel — an 8,071-foot passage (1.53 miles) through the mountain connecting the Poland and Walker mining districts. The tunnel was 8 feet tall and 12 feet wide — wide enough for the narrow-gauge ore cars. It operated until the Poland branch railroad was abandoned in 1939. The south portal collapsed in 1949 and is now sealed; the north (Walker) end still actively discharges water. Both the mill foundations and the sealed south portal are visible from Poland Road today, approximately 5–6 miles from AZ-69 before reaching Five Corners.

At the top of Poland Road — ~5.9 miles from AZ-69 — is Five Corners (elevation 6,926 ft), where five forest roads converge on the high plateau of Big Bug Mesa. This is the primary staging area for the Big Bug Mesa OHV and hiking trail network, and one of the defining geographic landmarks of the Poland Road corridor.

The Big Bug Mining District & Henrietta Mine

The Big Bug Mining District in the Bradshaw Mountains produced approximately 627,000 ounces of gold between 1867 and 1959 — making it one of Arizona's most historically productive mining areas. Other significant mines in the district include the Blue Bell and Iron King (Humboldt, ~$100 million total production before 1968 closure).

The Henrietta Mine lies within or immediately adjacent to the Goswick Ranch subdivision. Known variously as the Big Bug Mine (its pre-1866 name), American Flag Mine, and Braganza Gold Mine, it is one of the district's most significant and longest-active operations:

  • 1866 — Already ~50 feet deep and among the active mines in the district
  • 1883–1884 — "The most prominent mine in the district"
  • 1915–1919 — Big Ledge Copper Company dug a 2,200-foot tunnel; construction of the Mayer Smokestack (129.5 ft, built 1917) to carry smelter smoke eight miles from the workings to the town of Mayer
  • 1984–1986 — Poland Junction Mining Corp. operated a gravity mill, producing approximately $1.2 million in gold
  • 2022 — Arizona Department of Environmental Quality opened public comment for an air quality permit for crushing and screening operations at the site
  • 2023 — Arizona State Mine Inspector held a reclamation public meeting for Henrietta Mine LLC (18 active claims)

The Henrietta Mine's proximity is the primary reason arsenic testing of private wells is important for Goswick Ranch residents. The Big Bug district is a documented source of acid mine drainage and metal leaching into fractured bedrock aquifers. See Utilities for well testing resources.

The mining heritage also gives Goswick Ranch its character: the Mayer Smokestack — still standing in Mayer today — was built in direct service to a mine that neighbors the subdivision. The street names Miners Pick Rd and Elizabeth Mine Rd are named for the working history of this land.

The Land Itself

The Bradshaw Mountains that frame Goswick Ranch are among the oldest geological formations in the American Southwest. The granite and gneiss beneath the community's feet are more than 1.7 billion years old — formed long before complex life existed on Earth, shaped by forces that built the core of the North American continent.

The Kwevkapaya Yavapai people mined copper in these mountains from approximately 1100–1600 AD, long before European prospectors arrived. The land has seen cattle drives, mining booms, wildfires, and floods — and it has persisted through all of them.

The Streets Remember

The internal roads of Goswick Ranch carry names drawn from the mining and ranching heritage of the Bradshaw Mountains:

  • S Hawk Mountain Trail — The mountains themselves
  • S Miners Pick Rd — The tools of the mining era
  • E Vaca Bonita Trail — "Beautiful cow" — the ranching tradition
  • Bullhorn Rd — Cattle country
  • E Pearl Mountain Rd — The peaks of the Bradshaws
  • E Elizabeth Mine Rd — One of the district's historic mines

Each name is a small piece of the record — the geology, the animals, the working culture of people who came before.
