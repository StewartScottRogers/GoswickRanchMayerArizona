About Goswick Ranch

A private gated community on Poland Road in the Bradshaw Mountains — at nearly 6,000 feet, overlooking Big Bug Creek, with Prescott National Forest as a back door.

Community Overview

Goswick Ranch sits on Poland Road (E Poland Rd) in the foothills of the Bradshaw Mountains, Yavapai County, Arizona. It is a privately gated residential subdivision of approximately 25–35 individually titled lots, each a minimum of 9 acres, carved from the historic Goswick family ranchland overlooking the Big Bug Creek valley. The nearest town — Mayer, Arizona — is about one mile from where Poland Road meets AZ-69. The community entrance is marked by a custom ornamental iron gate with cowboy and cowgirl silhouette panels flanking a sun design — the ranch character announced before you're a foot inside.

The community's covenants set a 9-acre minimum lot size, ensuring that the open, rural character of the land is preserved. Homes must be site-built and at least 1,500 square feet — no manufactured or mobile homes. The result is a neighborhood that genuinely feels like ranch country, not a typical suburban development.

Residents enjoy dramatic mountain scenery, clean high-elevation air, and a level of privacy that's increasingly rare in the greater Phoenix–Prescott corridor. The HOA assessment is $216 per year — among the lowest you'll find for a gated, private-road community anywhere in Arizona. The internal road network is privately maintained by the HOA.

The Poland Road Corridor

Poland Road is more than the address of Goswick Ranch — it is the road into the Bradshaw Mountains. Beginning at AZ-69 near Poland Junction, it climbs ~5.9 miles through the Bradshaw foothills to Five Corners (elevation 6,926 ft), the hub of the Prescott National Forest trail and OHV network where five forest roads converge on the high plateau above. Big Bug Creek runs parallel to the road for most of that climb.

Residents who drive Poland Road regularly pass two of the Bradshaw Mountains' most significant historic landmarks, both visible from the road:

  • Poland Mine mill foundations (~5 miles up from AZ-69) — stone and concrete remnants of Frank Murphy's 20-stamp mill and concentrator, built in 1902. In 1907 alone, this mill produced $130,465 in gold and 16,609 ounces of silver. The mine vein produced primarily silver and gold 1900–1912.
  • The sealed south portal of the Poland-Walker Tunnel (~5–6 miles up) — the entrance to an 8,071-foot tunnel (1.53 miles) built in ~1903 to connect the Poland and Walker mining districts via narrow-gauge ore cars pulled by mules. The south portal collapsed in 1949 and is visible as a historic landmark from the road. The north end, in Walker, still actively discharges water.

This is a road with genuine history — named for David Robert Poland (1834–1882), who prospected the Bradshaws beginning in 1863, co-founded the New Era mine in 1870, and discovered the Poland Mine in 1872. He sold the mine to Chicago investors in October 1879 for approximately $3.5 million in modern terms. The road that now bears his name passes the physical remnants of what those investors built.

The Goswick Family & Ranch History

The ranch carries the name of a family that has been part of this landscape since the 1860s. William Isaac Goswick (1836–1904) worked as a mail carrier at Camp Verde during the frontier era. His descendant Giles Wesley Goswick (1888–1975) became a government lion hunter in the Bradshaw foothills — a real frontier role that involved tracking mountain lions for livestock protection across the range. George Allen Goswick (1919–1997) appears in the 1930 census living in the Mayer area, carrying the family's connection to this land into the twentieth century.

The family legacy continues today: Mark Goswick Cattle Co. still operates on Bloody Basin Road in the valley below the community (phone: 928-632-7730). You may see their cattle on open range. Under Arizona law, Yavapai County is open range — residents are responsible for fencing their property to keep livestock out, not the other way around.

The subdivision plat is recorded at the Yavapai County Recorder, Book 56, Pages 8–12, covering 850.63 acres in Sections 31 and 32, T 13 N, R 1 E. Streets within the community bear names that echo the area's mining and ranching heritage: S Hawk Mountain Trail, S Miners Pick Rd, E Vaca Bonita Trail, Bullhorn Rd, E Pearl Mountain Rd, and E Elizabeth Mine Rd.

The Bradshaw Mountains

Goswick Ranch sits in the foothills of the Bradshaw Mountains, a rugged range that runs roughly 40 miles north–south through central Yavapai County. The Bradshaws rise to 7,979 feet at Mount Union, their highest peak. The underlying rock is ancient — Precambrian granite and gneiss dating back 1.7 to 1.8 billion years, among the oldest exposed rock in Arizona.

Vegetation transitions sharply with elevation. At the community's lower elevations (~4,400 ft), alligator juniper, Emory oak, pointleaf manzanita, and cane cholla define the landscape — classic Madrean chaparral. At the upper range (~6,000 ft), ponderosa pine arrives and Gambel oak becomes common. Wildflower season peaks April through May: penstemon, lupine (purple and blue), globe mallow, and owl's clover across the hillsides. Fall color along the upper Senator Highway corridor is spectacular in October — bigtooth maple, Gambel oak, sumac, and cottonwood turning gold and red.

Prescott National Forest (Bradshaw Ranger District) essentially surrounds the community — it manages the mountains immediately above and beyond the gate, providing direct access to 450+ miles of trails for hiking, horseback riding, and dispersed camping.

Mining Heritage — The Big Bug District & Henrietta Mine

Goswick Ranch sits within the Big Bug Mining District, one of Arizona's most productive historic gold and silver districts. Between 1867 and 1959, the district yielded approximately 627,000 ounces of gold. The Henrietta Mine (USGS MRDS #10027183) lies within or immediately adjacent to the subdivision — it is among the district's most significant and longest-active mines, operating under multiple ownership eras from the 1860s through at least 2022.

At its peak in the 1915–1919 era under the Big Ledge Copper Company, the Henrietta Mine drove construction of a 2,200-foot tunnel and the Mayer Smokestack — a 129.5-foot industrial chimney built in the town of Mayer in 1917 to carry smelter smoke eight miles away from the mine workings. The smokestack still stands in Mayer today.

This mining heritage has two practical implications for residents: (1) the district geology is the primary reason elevated arsenic is found in local well water — private wells should be tested every five years; and (2) the street names within Goswick Ranch (S Miners Pick Rd, E Elizabeth Mine Rd) are not decorative — they reflect the actual character of this land's history. See Utilities for well water testing information.

Wildlife

The Bradshaw foothills support a diverse and sometimes dramatic mix of wildlife. Mule deer — not the white-tailed deer common in other parts of Arizona — are frequently seen throughout the year. Javelina (collared peccary) travel in herds of up to 20 and are common in the area; secure garbage and remove fallen fruit from trees, and never feed them. Mountain lions are present and active in the Bradshaws — the same terrain that made lion hunting a viable livelihood in Giles Goswick's day remains mountain lion habitat. If encountered: do not run; maintain eye contact; appear large; fight back if attacked. Black bears are confirmed in this elevation range, most active at dawn and dusk, and will raid bird feeders and fruit trees in drought years. Wild turkeys (Merriam's) are common at middle elevations. Coyotes are abundant; keep small pets (under 25 lbs) indoors at night.

Rattlesnakes at Goswick Ranch elevation include the black-tailed rattlesnake and the Arizona black rattlesnake — both documented in the Bradshaw Mountains. Active April–October; wear boots and long pants in brushy areas. Gila monsters are possible but rare above 5,000 feet.

The area sits in the chaparral-to-ponderosa transition zone, which concentrates bird diversity: golden eagles, acorn woodpeckers, Steller's jays, broad-tailed hummingbirds (the primary breeding hummingbird in the Bradshaws), and Mexican spotted owls are all recorded in this zone.

Dark Skies

Goswick Ranch sits under Bortle Class 3–4 skies — a designation reserved for genuinely dark locations where the Milky Way is visible as a brilliant, structured band, the Gegenschein is routinely visible, and faint nebulae can be seen without optical aid. Telescope or not, the night sky here is a world apart from anything the Phoenix metro area offers.

Peak viewing runs from October through early June; the July–September monsoon season brings cloud cover. The nearest significant light-polluting city, Prescott, is 27 miles away and its glow is largely blocked by the Bradshaw ridgeline to the west.

Big Bug Creek & Monsoon Season

The community overlooks Big Bug Creek, a seasonal-to-perennial tributary of the Agua Fria River that flows through a dramatic canyon below — flat sandstone rock formations, crystal-clear water in season, and cottonwood-lined banks. The creek drains a large watershed from the Bradshaw Mountains. Official monsoon season runs June 15–September 30; at Goswick Ranch elevation, monsoonal moisture typically first arrives in early July. Afternoon thunderstorms can be dramatic — intense localized rainfall, lightning, and Big Bug Creek rising from trickle to torrent in under an hour.

Post-fire flooding is a particular risk. After the 2017 Goodwin Fire burned 28,516 acres across the Bradshaws, hydrophobic soil in the burn scar sent a catastrophic flood down Big Bug Creek on July 19, 2017, damaging or destroying 109 homes in its path — from just 1–2 inches of rain in 30 minutes. See Emergency Services for current preparedness information.

Location in Context

Goswick Ranch is ~1.5 miles up Poland Road from AZ-69, placing it at the foot of the Bradshaw Mountains. Mayer, the nearest town, is approximately 2 miles from the gate. Prescott is 27 miles northwest via SR-69 (~35 minutes); the north Phoenix metro area is approximately 75 miles south via I-17. Interstate 17 is accessible via Cordes Junction, about 15 miles east. Sky Harbor Airport is roughly 70 minutes via I-17. Sedona is about 60 miles northeast.

Mayer — The Nearest Town

Mayer is a small, unincorporated community in Yavapai County at 4,695 feet elevation — approximately one mile from where Poland Road meets AZ-69. It is not the identity of Goswick Ranch; it is the closest supply point. Gas, grocery, hardware, a post office, a pharmacy, two medical clinics, and a public library are all in Mayer or the immediate Spring Valley corridor. The annual "Mayer Daze" community festival — held each October since the early 1960s — is a genuine small-town celebration with parade, classic cars, BBQ, and live entertainment.

For larger stores (Walmart, Costco, Target), major medical services, and full city amenities, Prescott Valley is 13 miles south (~20 minutes) and Prescott is 27 miles southwest (~35 minutes).

Mayer was founded in 1882 by Joseph Hoffmayer (who went by Joseph Mayer), a gold prospector from New York. His wife, Sarah Belle Wilbur, became the town's first postmistress in 1884. Three Mayer structures are listed on the National Register of Historic Places: the Mayer Apartments, the Mayer Business Block, and the Mayer Red Brick Schoolhouse.

Real Estate Context

Yavapai County is one of the fastest-growing counties in the nation — growing at roughly five times the national rate, driven entirely by in-migration. Property values in the Goswick Ranch area have appreciated approximately 57% over the five-year period from 2020 to 2026. Active listings as of March 2026 ranged from $275,000 (vacant 9-acre land) to $750,000 (land with original ranch buildings). The median property tax in this ZIP code is around $476 per year — dramatically lower than the national median of $2,400 — reflecting Arizona's 0.44% effective property tax rate.

Arizona has no state income tax on Social Security benefits and a flat 2.5% income tax rate, making it a financially attractive destination for retirees and remote workers. Starlink satellite internet, confirmed available at ZIP 86333, has expanded the buyer pool to include working-age professionals who can work remotely from anywhere.
