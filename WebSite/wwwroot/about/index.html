About Goswick Ranch

An overview of the community, its setting, and what makes it distinctive.

Community Overview

Goswick Ranch is a privately gated residential subdivision in Mayer, Arizona — a small community of ranch homes perched in the foothills of the Bradshaw Mountains, overlooking Big Bug Creek valley.

The community's covenants set a 9-acre minimum lot size, ensuring that the open, rural character of the land is preserved. Homes must be site-built and at least 1,500 square feet — no manufactured or mobile homes. The result is a neighborhood that genuinely feels like ranch country, not a typical suburban development.

Residents enjoy dramatic mountain scenery, clean high-desert air, and a level of privacy that's increasingly rare in the greater Phoenix–Prescott corridor. The HOA assessment is $216 per year — among the lowest you'll find for a gated, private-road community anywhere in Arizona.

The Goswick Family & Ranch History

The ranch carries the name of a family that has been part of this landscape since the 1860s. William Isaac Goswick (1836–1904) worked as a mail carrier out of Camp Verde during the territorial era. His son Giles Wesley Goswick (1888–1975) became a government lion hunter — a real frontier role that involved tracking mountain lions for livestock protection across the Bradshaw range. George Allen Goswick (1919–1997) appears in the 1930 census living in the Mayer area, carrying the family's connection to this land into the twentieth century.

The family legacy continues today: Mark Goswick Cattle Co. still operates on Bloody Basin Road in the valley below the community. You may see their cattle on open range. Under Arizona law, Yavapai County is open range — residents are responsible for fencing their property to keep livestock out, not the other way around.

The subdivision plat is recorded at the Yavapai County Recorder, Book 56, Pages 8–12. Streets within the community bear names that echo the area's mining and ranching heritage: S Hawk Mountain Trail, S Miners Pick Rd, E Vaca Bonita Trail, Bullhorn Rd, E Pearl Mountain Rd, and E Elizabeth Mine Rd.

The Bradshaw Mountains

Goswick Ranch sits in the foothills of the Bradshaw Mountains, a rugged range that runs roughly 40 miles north–south through central Yavapai County. The Bradshaws rise to 7,979 feet at Mount Union, their highest peak. The underlying rock is ancient — Precambrian granite and gneiss dating back 1.7 to 1.8 billion years, among the oldest exposed rock in Arizona.

Vegetation transitions sharply with elevation. At the community's lower elevations, alligator juniper, Emory oak, pointleaf manzanita, and cane cholla define the landscape — classic Madrean chaparral. Above 5,500 feet, ponderosa pine takes over. Wildflower season peaks in April and May (penstemon, lupine, globe mallow, and owl's clover), and fall color along the upper Senator Highway corridor is spectacular in October — bigtooth maple, Gambel oak, sumac, and cottonwood turning gold and red.

The range has a rich mining history — the Walker Mining District, just to the north, was one of Arizona's most productive gold and silver areas in the late 1800s. Today the Bradshaws are managed by Prescott National Forest and offer more than 450 miles of trails for hiking, horseback riding, and dispersed camping.

Wildlife

The Bradshaw foothills support a diverse and sometimes dramatic mix of wildlife. Mule deer (not the white-tailed deer common in other parts of Arizona) are frequently seen in the area. Mountain lions are present and active — the same terrain that made lion hunting a viable livelihood in Giles Goswick's day remains mountain lion habitat today. Wild turkeys are common at middle elevations. Black-tailed rattlesnakes and Arizona black rattlesnakes have been documented at elevations up to 6,000 feet; Gila monsters are rare above 5,000 feet but possible. Residents should also watch for coatimundi, javelina, and black bears during warmer months.

The area sits in the chaparral-to-ponderosa transition zone, which concentrates biodiversity. Birding is excellent: zone-tailed hawks, Mexican jays, acorn woodpeckers, and Steller's jays are common. The area has not yet been impacted by buffelgrass, the invasive grass that has devastated lower-elevation Sonoran Desert habitats.

Dark Skies

Goswick Ranch sits under Bortle Class 3 skies — a designation reserved for genuinely dark locations where the Milky Way is visible as a brilliant, structured band, the Gegenschein is routinely visible, and faint nebulae can be seen without optical aid. Telescope or not, the night sky here is a world apart from anything the Phoenix metro area offers.

Peak viewing runs from October through June; the July–September monsoon season brings cloud cover. The nearest light-polluting city, Prescott, is 27 miles away and its glow is largely blocked by the Bradshaw ridgeline to the west.

Big Bug Creek & Monsoon Season

The community overlooks Big Bug Creek, a seasonal tributary of the Agua Fria River that flows through a dramatic canyon below. The creek drains a large watershed from the Bradshaw Mountains. During monsoon season (July–September), Arizona's dramatic convective thunderstorms build rapidly — afternoon cells can produce intense localized rainfall, and Big Bug Creek can rise from trickle to torrent in less than an hour.

The monsoon trigger typically develops between 2 and 4 PM; Arizona generates more than 500,000 lightning strikes per season. Residents should be aware of flash flood potential during heavy rain events. Post-fire flooding is a particular risk — after the 2017 Goodwin Fire burned 28,516 acres across the Bradshaws, hydrophobic soil in the burn scar sent a catastrophic flood down Big Bug Creek on July 19, 2017, damaging or destroying 109 homes in its path. See Emergency Services for current preparedness information.

Location in Context

Mayer, Arizona sits between Prescott (about 27 miles northwest via SR-69) and the north Phoenix metro area (about 75 miles south via I-17). Interstate 17 is accessible via Cordes Junction, about 15 miles east. The drive to downtown Prescott takes about 40 minutes; Sky Harbor Airport is roughly 70 minutes via I-17. Sedona is about 60 miles northeast.

About Mayer

Mayer is a small, unincorporated community in Yavapai County with a population of about 1,558 (2020 Census). Sitting at approximately 4,695 feet elevation, it offers genuine four seasons — warm summers with afternoon monsoon storms, colorful falls, crisp winters with occasional snow, and mild springs. Average high in summer is 91°F; snowfall averages about 19 inches per year.

The town was founded in 1882 by Joseph Hoffmayer (who went by Joseph Mayer), a gold prospector and entrepreneur from New York. His wife, Sarah Belle Wilbur, became the town's first postmistress in 1884. The Southern Pacific Railroad arrived in 1898, cementing Mayer's role as a supply hub for the Bradshaw Mountains mining district.

Three Mayer structures are listed on the National Register of Historic Places: the Mayer Apartments, the Mayer Business Block, and the Mayer Red Brick Schoolhouse. The annual "Mayer Daze" community festival — held each October since the early 1960s — celebrates the town's heritage with a parade, classic cars, BBQ, vendors, and live entertainment.

Real Estate Context

Yavapai County is one of the fastest-growing counties in the nation — growing at roughly five times the national rate. Property values in the Goswick Ranch area have appreciated approximately 57% over the five-year period from 2020 to 2026. The median property tax on Goswick Ranch-area properties is around $476 per year — dramatically lower than the national median of $2,400 — reflecting Arizona's 0.44% effective property tax rate.

Arizona has no state income tax on Social Security benefits and a flat 2.5% income tax rate, making it a financially attractive destination for retirees and remote workers. The combination of low HOA fees ($216/year), low property taxes, and Bortle Class 3 dark skies at 5,000+ feet elevation is genuinely unusual.
