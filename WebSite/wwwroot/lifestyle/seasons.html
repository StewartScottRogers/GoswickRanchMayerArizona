Seasons, Climate & Dark Skies

Genuine four-season living at 4,400–6,000 feet — and one of the darkest night skies within two hours of Phoenix.

Climate Overview

At 4,400–6,000 feet, Goswick Ranch escapes the brutal summer heat that defines the Phoenix experience. Annual temperatures range from roughly 31°F in the coldest winter nights to 92°F on the hottest summer days. The average growing season runs approximately 235 days (late March through mid-November). At Goswick Ranch's ~6,000 ft upper elevation, summer highs run 10–15°F cooler than Phoenix.

Annual precipitation in the Mayer area is around 10 inches of rain and 19 inches of snow. Snow typically arrives as light dustings that melt within a day or two, though higher elevations see more accumulation. Clearest month: September (83% clear or mostly clear).

Four Seasons

Spring (March–May)

Spring at elevation means crisp mornings, warm afternoons, and the gradual emergence of the Bradshaw wildflower bloom. April through May bring the main wildflower window at ~6,000 ft. Key species: lupine (purple and blue, May–June), penstemon (hummingbird magnets), clarkia, sacred datura (large white), globe mallow, owl's clover, and desert marigold. In the remarkable 2025–2026 season, wildflowers appeared as early as January — the earliest in 15 years.

Track real-time wildflower reports: AZ State Parks Wildflower Bloomdar.

Summer / Monsoon (June–September)

Official Arizona monsoon season runs June 15–September 30. At Goswick Ranch elevation, monsoonal moisture typically first arrives in early July. Afternoon thunderstorms build over the Bradshaws, often triggering between 2:00–4:00 PM. Lightning is dramatic and frequent — Arizona sees over 500,000 lightning strikes per monsoon season. Rain can be intense: 1–2 inches in 30 minutes is enough to send Big Bug Creek from trickle to torrent.

The petrichor of desert rain on chaparral — creosote oil and geosmin released by rainfall — is one of the most beloved smells in the natural world. For residents, this means beautiful storm-watching from a high vantage point. It also means vigilance: Big Bug Creek can rise catastrophically after any fire burn scar upstream. Never attempt to cross flooded roads or washes.

Summer temperatures at Goswick Ranch are pleasant by Arizona standards — highs in the upper 80s to low 90s rather than the 110°F+ Phoenix endures.

Fall (October–November)

Fall brings the best driving season. The Senator Highway south toward Crown King is a premier fall-color route through the Bradshaws. Oak and sycamore in the creek drainages turn amber and rust. Temperatures drop into ideal hiking range. Fall color peak in the Bradshaw/Prescott area: mid-October through early November.

Best fall color destinations within an hour:

  • Goldwater Lake (Bradshaws, south of Prescott) — aspens, cottonwoods, maple, ash; brilliant yellow, orange, and gold
  • Willow Lake Park (Prescott) — cottonwoods along the western edge; Granite Dells backdrop
  • SR-89A scenic corridor (Prescott to Sedona via Jerome and Mingus Mountain) — one of the finest fall drives in the state

Winter (December–February)

Light snow falls on average about 16 days per year in the Mayer area. It rarely accumulates more than a few inches and typically melts within a day or two. Cold nights and bright, crisp days make winter at Goswick Ranch genuinely beautiful — Bortle Class 3–4 skies with exceptional clarity, Orion and the Pleiades blazing overhead, and mornings that feel like a different Arizona entirely from anything in the Phoenix valley.

Dark Skies — Bortle Class 3–4

Goswick Ranch sits in a Bortle Class 3–4 dark sky zone — among the darkest designations achievable within driving distance of any major Arizona city. On a clear night from October through early June (outside monsoon season), the Milky Way is not just visible — it is brilliant, arcing from horizon to horizon with the naked eye. The Gegenschein is routinely visible; faint nebulae can be detected without optical aid; limiting naked-eye magnitude reaches approximately 6.5 (thousands of stars).

This level of darkness results from the combination of elevation, distance from Prescott and Phoenix light domes, and the natural terrain blocking ambient glow from lower valleys. For residents interested in amateur astronomy, astrophotography, or simply sitting outside at night, Goswick Ranch offers conditions that photographers and astronomers specifically seek out.

Best viewing months: October–November (excellent, low humidity, summer Milky Way visible early evening); January–February (cold but very transparent; Orion, Pleiades); March–May (galaxy season). Avoid July–September (monsoon clouds).

Resources: Prescott Clear Sky Chart · Prescott Astronomy Club (monthly star parties, ~28 miles away)

Wildlife

The Bradshaw Mountains and Big Bug Creek drainage support a diverse community of wildlife that regularly visits and crosses through Goswick Ranch. The chaparral-to-ponderosa transition zone at this elevation concentrates biodiversity in a way that's rare in Arizona.

Commonly Observed

  • Mule deer — The dominant deer species in the Bradshaws; frequently seen throughout the year; bucks visible during November–December rut
  • Wild turkeys (Merriam's) — Year-round residents at middle elevations; flocks common in fall and winter; ground feeders in ponderosa/oak woodland
  • Coyotes — Abundant at all elevations; heard nightly; active at dawn and dusk; keep pets under 25 lbs indoors at night
  • Jackrabbits and cottontails
  • Raptors — Golden eagle, red-tailed hawk, Cooper's hawk, northern goshawk, American kestrel, and peregrine falcon year-round
  • Hummingbirds — Broad-tailed hummingbird (primary breeding species in the Bradshaws, April–August); Anna's hummingbird (year-round); black-chinned and rufous (spring/summer migrants)
  • Woodpeckers — Acorn woodpecker (abundant in oak zones), hairy woodpecker, ladder-backed woodpecker
  • Jays — Steller's jay and common raven year-round

Less Frequently Seen

  • Mountain lions — Present and active in the Bradshaws; multiple documented incidents in Prescott area 2023–2024. The same terrain where Giles Wesley Goswick worked as a government lion hunter remains mountain lion habitat today. If encountered: do NOT run; maintain eye contact; appear large; fight back if attacked; target eyes and nose. Protect small pets and livestock outdoors at night.
  • Black bears — Confirmed in the Bradshaws (primary habitat 4,000–10,000 ft; Goswick Ranch is squarely within bear range). Shy, most active dawn/dusk; will raid bird feeders, garbage, and fruit trees during drought years. Report encounters: AZGFD (928) 692-7700.
  • Javelina (collared peccary) — Herd animals (up to 20); 35–60 lbs; active dawn/dusk/night. Common in the area. Charge when cornered or when dogs are present. Never feed javelina. Secure garbage; remove fallen fruit; single-strand electric fencing (8–10 inches above ground) is effective for gardens.
  • Coatimundi — Rare but documented in central Arizona highlands

Venomous Species

Two rattlesnake species are specifically documented in the Bradshaw Mountains at Goswick Ranch elevations:

  • Black-tailed rattlesnake (Crotalus molossus) — Primary species at this elevation; favors mountainous terrain; black tail distinctive
  • Arizona black rattlesnake (Crotalus cerberus) — Documented specifically in the Bradshaw Mountains

Active April–October; can be encountered on warm winter days. Wear boots and long pants in brushy areas. If bitten: go to the ER immediately; do NOT cut, suck, or apply a tourniquet.

Gila monsters are possible but rare at 6,000 ft (typical upper range ~5,000 ft, with exceptions). Slow-moving; venomous; leave alone if encountered.

Native Plants

The plant community transitions from high-desert chaparral (pointleaf manzanita, scrub live oak, desert broom, Apache plume) at lower elevations to piñon-juniper woodland and ponderosa pine toward the higher Bradshaws. Dominant trees in the chaparral zone include alligator juniper, Emory oak, and Arizona white oak; above 5,500 ft, ponderosa pine arrives as the dominant species. Soils are predominantly Precambrian granite 1.7–1.8 billion years old — shallow, rocky, and rapid-draining.

Note: Saguaro cactus is NOT present at 6,000 ft. Saguaro's upper limit is approximately 4,500–5,000 ft. What you will find: prickly pear, cane cholla, agave, and banana yucca throughout the chaparral zone.
