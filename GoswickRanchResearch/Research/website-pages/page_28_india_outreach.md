## Critical Legal Considerations

**These must be addressed clearly. NRI buyers in particular are sophisticated about US legal and tax matters.**

### Property Ownership on a Work Visa
- **H-1B, L-1, O-1, and most other work visa holders can legally purchase US real estate.** Visa status does not restrict real estate ownership.
- Green card holders and US citizens face no restriction whatsoever.
- The concern some buyers have — that purchasing property could complicate their immigration status — is generally unfounded. Property ownership does not create or destroy immigration rights.
- If a visa holder sells and leaves the US, FIRPTA rules apply as for any foreign person.

### FEMA and RBI Rules for India-Resident Buyers
- Indian residents (not NRIs/OCIs) are subject to Reserve Bank of India (RBI) and Foreign Exchange Management Act (FEMA) regulations on outward remittances.
- Under the Liberalised Remittance Scheme (LRS), resident Indians can remit up to **USD 250,000 per financial year** per person. A couple can remit $500,000/yr under LRS.
- For property purchases above this threshold, RBI approval may be required, or a multi-year LRS approach used. Consult a FEMA-specialist CA (Chartered Accountant) in India before transacting.
- **NRIs and OCIs are not subject to LRS restrictions** on funds already held outside India or earned abroad.

### US Tax on Indian Buyers
- Non-resident alien buyers are subject to FIRPTA (15% withholding on gross sale price at future sale, credited against actual US tax liability).
- US estate tax exposure applies to US-sited property owned by non-US persons. The US and India **do not have a comprehensive bilateral estate tax treaty**; planning is essential.
- The US–India Income Tax Treaty exists and provides some relief on income tax, but estate tax is not covered.
- NRIs who are US tax residents (green card holders, substantial presence test) are taxed on worldwide income — different rules apply.

## Cultural Sensitivities

### DO
- **Address the NRI segment first and specifically.** "NRI" and "OCI" are terms the Indian audience knows. Using them signals that this page was written for them, not retrofitted.
- **Be direct about numbers.** Indian buyers — especially those from tech and finance — respond well to specific data: $692/yr carrying cost, $216 HOA, $476 property tax. Lead with facts.
- **Acknowledge the green card backlog reality.** Indian nationals in the H-1B pipeline face decade-long waits for green cards. For many, owning US property is both a financial investment and a psychological commitment to their US life. Acknowledge this without condescension.
- **Multi-generational framing resonates.** Indian buyers often think in family terms — property for themselves and their parents, or that their children will inherit. The 9-acre minimum lot and the ability to build a main house and guest quarters (subject to permits) is worth noting.
- **Climate familiarity.** Many Indian buyers from metro areas respond well to Arizona's warm, sunny weather as a contrast to cold winters in northern US cities (Chicago, New York, Seattle).
- **Note the Phoenix Indian-American community.** Chandler, Tempe, and Scottsdale have a large, established Indian-American community with cultural organizations, temples, grocery stores, and restaurants. Proximity matters for weekend/vacation use.

### DON'T
- Don't treat the India-resident buyer and the US-based NRI as the same audience — they have different constraints, motivations, and purchasing logistics.
- Don't ignore the RBI/LRS/FEMA issue for India-resident buyers — it's real and a common blocker. Address it plainly.
- Don't make assumptions about religion, caste, or regional origin.
- Don't overlook that many NRIs are highly informed about US real estate and will catch vague or imprecise language about taxes or ownership rules.
