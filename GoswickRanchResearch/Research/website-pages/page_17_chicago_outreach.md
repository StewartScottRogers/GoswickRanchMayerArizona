# Chicago / Midwest Outreach — Culturally Sensitive Retirement Pitch
### Working Draft — April 2026

*Status: DRAFT — working document; not yet approved for site*
*Purpose: Work out messaging for Chicago metropolitan area and broader Midwest retirees. Distinct from both the NYC and California audiences — more working-class in cultural identity, more directly weather-motivated, and less likely to already know Arizona as a destination.*

---

## What We're Trying to Do

Chicago and the Midwest present a different kind of opportunity than NYC or California. The financial pain points are real but less extreme. The cultural identity is different — more working-class, more practical, less cosmopolitan. The weather motivation is, frankly, enormous and can be named directly.

The Chicago audience does not need as much cultural bridging as the NYC audience. They are more likely to have rural roots (many Chicago families are one or two generations off the farm), more likely to find ranch culture intuitive, and more likely to respond to straight talk over polished copy.

**The right outcome:** A retired Chicago firefighter, a retired auto worker from Indiana, a retired nurse from the suburbs who has been talking about "getting out of this weather" for twenty years reads this and thinks *"This is real. This is affordable. We should drive out and look."*

## Target Audience: Chicago / Midwest Retirees

### Who We're Talking To

**Primary Chicago metro:**
- City of Chicago (all neighborhoods; note cultural variation from Northside to Southside to working-class West Side)
- Cook County suburbs (Evanston, Naperville, Schaumburg, Oak Park, Orland Park)
- DuPage County (wealthier suburban corridor; Wheaton, Downers Grove, Elmhurst)
- Lake County, IL; Will County, IL

**Broader Midwest secondary markets:**
- Milwaukee / Madison WI
- Detroit metro (MI)
- Indianapolis (IN)
- Columbus, Cincinnati, Cleveland (OH)
- Minneapolis–St. Paul (MN) — extremely winter-motivated
- Kansas City, St. Louis (MO)

**Key characteristics of this audience:**

| Pressure | Chicago / Midwest Reality |
|---|---|
| Winters | Chicago: brutal; average 35 inches of snow; wind chill well below 0°F; 5–6 months of cold/grey; Minneapolis is worse |
| Property taxes | Cook County is among the highest in the US: Chicago average ~$6,000–$10,000/yr; collar counties vary |
| State income tax | Illinois: 4.95% flat; Wisconsin: up to 7.65%; Michigan: 4.25%; Minnesota: up to 9.85% |
| Illinois fiscal situation | Illinois has among the worst pension liabilities of any US state; some retirees are factoring state fiscal stability into decisions |
| Cost of living | Not NYC/SF extreme, but Chicago is above national average; housing in nicer suburbs is expensive |
| Cultural identity | Strong working-class roots; union culture; family-oriented; Midwest nice; practical; skeptical of hype |

### What Makes This Audience Different

The Midwest audience is less financially motivated than NYC or California, but more **weather-motivated** than any other audience. Chicago winters are a legitimate daily-life burden for aging residents. Joint problems, driving on ice, heating costs, months of darkness — this is not abstract. It's physical.

The messaging can name the weather directly in a way that would feel too blunt with a California audience but lands as common sense with a Chicagoan.

## Cultural Sensitivities to Navigate

### DO

- **Name the winter.** It is the elephant in the room and every Chicagoan knows it. Treating the weather angle as the primary driver is not condescending — it's honest.
- **Respect working-class identity.** Chicago and the broader Midwest have deep blue-collar roots. Ranch life — fencing, well water, propane tanks, land maintenance — is not exotic or foreign. It's practical. Frame it as such.
- **Acknowledge rural roots.** A significant portion of the Chicago-area retirement population is one or two generations removed from Illinois, Indiana, or Iowa farms. The land-use culture at Goswick Ranch (livestock, horses, agricultural use) may feel more like a return than a departure.
- **Lead with value.** Midwesterners are value-oriented. The $216/year HOA is a headline fact for this audience. Don't bury it.
- **Mention the drive option.** Chicago to Mayer, AZ is approximately 1,650 miles — a long but doable road trip. Many Midwest retirees will want to drive out and see it rather than fly. The Route 66 / I-40 corridor is familiar and even nostalgic for this generation.
- **Be direct.** Chicago culture is straightforward. Long, polished copy with emotional setup reads as sales pitch. Short, factual sentences with real numbers work better.

### DON'T

- Don't over-romanticize the ranching lifestyle. A retired steelworker from the South Side has seen more BS than most. Present facts and let them conclude.
- Don't use coastal cultural references. Wine country, tech sector equity, Manhattan real estate — these don't land. Use working numbers and real comparisons.
- Don't ignore Illinois fiscal concerns if they come up. Some Illinois retirees are leaving partly because of concern about the state's long-term pension situation affecting taxes. This is a legitimate factor — you don't need to argue it, just don't pretend it isn't there.
- Don't assume everyone from Chicago is from the city proper. Collar county and suburban residents have their own identity — more affluent in DuPage, more working-class in Will/Lake. The messaging should work for both.
- Don't forget Minneapolis and Wisconsin. Winters there are even more severe. The weather angle lands even harder.

## Midwest-Specific Considerations

### The Drive-Out Option
Many Midwest retirees will want to drive to Arizona before committing to a purchase. The Chicago → Mayer route:
- I-55 S → I-44 → I-40 W (old Route 66 corridor) → I-17 S → SR 69 E
- Approximately 1,650 miles; 2–3 day drive; a trip many Midwesterners have made in some variation
- Road trip culture is deeply embedded in Midwest retirement planning — lean into this

Consider: "Drive the old Route 66 corridor and stop in Mayer on the way" is a pitch that resonates with a 65-year-old from Chicago in a way that "book a flight" does not.

### Illinois Fiscal Climate
Illinois has documented pension underfunding issues. Property tax increases in Cook County have accelerated. Some retirees are specifically leaving Illinois because of concern about long-term tax trajectory. We don't need to editorialize on this — just don't be oblivious to it. If it comes up in context, the Arizona comparison speaks for itself.

### Minneapolis and Wisconsin
Minneapolis retirees have even more weather motivation than Chicagoans. Minnesota's state income tax (up to 9.85%) is also significantly higher than Arizona's. The financial and weather case is, if anything, stronger for a Minneapolis buyer. This content works for the broader Midwest with no changes needed.

### Indiana, Michigan, Ohio
These states have more moderate tax environments than Illinois or Minnesota, but the winters and cost-of-living pressures are still real. For this audience, the weather and the land value story are the primary pitch.

#### H3: Property Taxes — Cook County vs. Yavapai County

The Cook County median property tax is approximately $7,000 per year. On a typical Chicago suburban home it runs higher.

Goswick Ranch property taxes average approximately $476 per year on 9-acre mountain parcels. Some vacant land parcels carry annual taxes as low as $5.

The difference — roughly $6,500 per year — held over a 20-year retirement is $130,000. After tax.

#### H3: State Income Tax — Illinois vs. Arizona

Illinois taxes income at 4.95% flat. Arizona taxes it at 2.5% flat. On $70,000 in annual retirement income, that's approximately $1,750 per year in your pocket that Illinois would keep. On $100,000, it's $2,450.

Neither state taxes Social Security income. Arizona has no estate tax. Illinois applies an estate tax above $4 million.

**Minnesota buyers:** Your state income tax goes up to 9.85%. The Arizona comparison is more dramatic still.

### H2: How to Visit — Plan a Scouting Trip from Chicago

Before you decide anything, come see it. Midwest buyers tend to drive. This trip is worth the road.

**By car — the Route 66 corridor:**

The drive from Chicago to Goswick Ranch is approximately 1,650 miles — two to three days, via the old Route 66 corridor.

**Suggested route:** I-55 South from Chicago to St. Louis → I-44 West through Joplin and Oklahoma City → I-40 West through Amarillo and Albuquerque → I-17 South at Flagstaff → SR 69 East at Cordes Junction → E Poland Road.

This route through Albuquerque and Flagstaff passes some of the most dramatic scenery in the American Southwest. Many buyers make this into a two-week trip — Sedona, the Grand Canyon corridor — with Goswick Ranch as the destination. It's the right way to understand what Arizona is.

**By air:**

O'Hare (ORD) and Midway (MDW) both offer nonstop service to Phoenix Sky Harbor (PHX). Flight time is approximately 3.5 hours. Multiple airlines, multiple daily departures.

From Phoenix Sky Harbor: take I-17 North to Exit 262 (SR 69 East), then approximately 12 miles to Mayer. Total drive: approximately 70 minutes.

**Where to stay:**

Prescott, AZ (27 miles from the ranch) is the recommended base. Historic downtown Courthouse Plaza, walkable, good restaurants. The drive from Prescott to Goswick Ranch on SR 69 takes about 35 minutes and gives you a feel for the full corridor.

### H2: Frequently Asked Questions

**How much cheaper is it to retire in Arizona than Illinois?**

Illinois income tax is 4.95%; Arizona's is 2.5% flat. Cook County median property taxes are ~$7,000/yr vs. ~$476/yr at Goswick Ranch. **How long is the drive from Chicago to Goswick Ranch?**

Approximately 1,650 miles — typically a 2 to 3 day road trip via I-55 S / I-44 W / I-40 W (Route 66 corridor), then I-17 S to SR 69 E. Phoenix Sky Harbor is 74 miles from the community — 3.5 hours nonstop from O'Hare or Midway.

**What are the property taxes at Goswick Ranch?**

**Does Arizona tax Social Security income?**

No. Arizona does not tax Social Security income. Illinois also exempts Social Security, but Arizona's broader income tax rate of 2.5% flat is lower than Illinois' 4.95% on all other retirement income.
