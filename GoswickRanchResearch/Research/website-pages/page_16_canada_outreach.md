## What We're Trying to Do

Canada is different from the other regional pitches in one important way: **the Arizona snowbird pipeline already exists and is enormous.** An estimated 90,000–120,000 Canadians winter in Arizona annually. Goswick Ranch is not introducing Canadians to Arizona — it's showing them a less-known option within a state they already use seasonally.

The pitch for Canadians is therefore **snowbird-to-owner**, potentially building toward permanent relocation for some. The tone should acknowledge where most Canadian snowbirds start (seasonal renter, resort community) and present Goswick Ranch as the ownership path for those ready to own real land.

**The right outcome:** A retired couple from Mississauga, a retired engineer from Calgary, a retired teacher from Victoria who has been renting a Scottsdale condo every winter for five years reads this and thinks *"We could own land instead of renting. And at these prices."*

## Critical Legal and Financial Considerations

**These are unique to Canadian buyers and must be addressed directly. Ignoring them signals that we don't understand this audience.**

### 1. Visa and Residency

Canadians can enter the US as visitors (B-2 visa / ESTA not required for Canadians) and stay up to **6 months per calendar year** without a visa. This is the legal basis for snowbirding.

For **full-time relocation**, Canadians would need a US immigration pathway:
- Investor visas (EB-5)
- Employment-based visas (if applicable)
- TN visa (CUSMA/USMCA — for certain professional categories)
- Marriage to a US citizen

**Important framing:** Most of our Canadian audience is likely at the snowbird stage, not the full-relocation stage. Our pitch should not assume they're abandoning Canada — it should work for seasonal ownership.

**Action item:** Confirm with board whether Goswick Ranch CC&Rs permit seasonal (non-full-time) occupancy as a primary use. If yes, this is a major selling point for Canadians.

### 2. US Estate Tax Exposure

Canadians who own US property are subject to **US federal estate tax** on the value of that US property at death (2024 rate: 40% above the exemption). The Canada–US Tax Treaty provides relief, but Canadian buyers need to be aware and get professional advice.

> **Note for copy:** Don't explain this on the site — that's beyond HOA scope. But include a simple callout: *"Canadian buyers should consult a cross-border tax advisor before purchasing US property. There are important estate and income tax considerations unique to Canadian ownership."*

### 3. Currency

At C$1.35/USD (approximate), a $275,000 USD lot is ~C$371,250. A $450,000 USD lot is ~C$607,500. This is still dramatically cheaper than comparable land in BC or Ontario, but the currency conversion should be acknowledged — not avoided. Canadians are used to doing this math.

### 4. FIRPTA

Foreign buyers (including Canadians) are subject to FIRPTA withholding on the eventual sale (15% of gross sale price withheld at closing, credited against actual tax liability). Mention only in the "what to know" section — not in the pitch.

## Cultural Sensitivities to Navigate

### DO

- **Acknowledge the snowbird tradition.** This is not an insult — it's an identity. Many Canadians are proud of their Arizona winters. Lead with "you already know Arizona."
- **Speak to Alberta's ranch culture.** Alberta has the closest cultural overlap with a working ranch community — cattle, horses, wide land, practical self-reliance. For this audience, Goswick Ranch is not foreign. It's familiar.
- **Respect Canadian directness.** Canadians (especially from outside Toronto) tend to respond well to straight talk. The "honest picture" voice on the existing site works very well for this audience.
- **Acknowledge the healthcare question.** Canadians who consider full-time US residency face losing provincial health coverage. This is a real concern. Don't pretend it isn't. The soft answer: Arizona has good healthcare infrastructure and US supplemental insurance options for retirees exist — but this is a question to research.
- **Note the AZ snowbird infrastructure.** Arizona has been hosting Canadians for 40+ years. Snowbird-specific services, Canadian-friendly communities, and even Canadian-oriented real estate agents exist in the Phoenix metro.

### DON'T

- Don't use "escape Canada" framing. Canadians have strong national identity and most are not escaping — they're choosing a seasonal lifestyle.
- Don't ignore the currency conversion. If you quote prices without acknowledging the exchange rate, it reads as either naive or deceptive.
- Don't assume all Canadians speak English primarily. Quebec is a large market but needs separate French-language consideration (flag for future).
- Don't make this sound like a full-relocation pitch if seasonal ownership is more realistic for most buyers.
- Don't minimize the healthcare concern — it's genuine and important.

### H2: Can Canadians Own Property in Arizona?

Yes. Canadian citizens can own US real estate without restriction. There is no requirement to be a US resident or citizen to purchase property in Arizona.

#### H3: Visitor Stay Rules — The 6-Month Question

Canadian citizens can enter the United States and stay up to **6 months (183 days) per calendar year** without a visa, under the B-2 visitor category. This is the legal foundation of the Canadian snowbird lifestyle and it is well-established.

Staying longer than 183 days in a calendar year may have tax residency implications in both countries. If you are considering extended stays, consult a cross-border tax advisor.

Goswick Ranch has no minimum occupancy requirement. You can use the property for 6 months a year, for 2 months a year, or hold it without visiting at all. The $216 USD/year HOA applies regardless of how much time you spend here.

#### H3: Part-Time, Seasonal, or Investment — All Permitted

There is no rule requiring you to live at Goswick Ranch full-time. The ownership structure is straightforward:

- **Seasonal use:** Come for the Arizona winter, return to Canada for the summer. Many owners will find the 80s°F summers at Goswick Ranch preferable to Phoenix, but still choose to spend summers in Canada.
- **Investment only:** Purchase land with no immediate plan to occupy. Hold it as an appreciating asset in a corridor that Yavapai County planning documents identify as a growth area.
- **Future retirement base:** Purchase now, build when the time is right.

All of these are valid uses under the CC&Rs.

#### H3: What Canadian Buyers Need to Know Before Purchasing

US property ownership involves specific considerations for Canadian citizens. We are a homeowners association, not a legal or tax advisory service — but we want you to go into this with your eyes open.

> **A note for Canadian buyers:**
>
> Before purchasing US real estate, Canadian citizens should consult a cross-border tax attorney or accountant (often called a "snowbird advisor" in Canada) regarding:
>
> - **FIRPTA:** 15% of the gross sale price is withheld at the time of future sale and credited against your actual US tax liability. This is standard for all foreign buyers.
> - **US estate tax exposure:** US-sited property owned by Canadian citizens may be subject to US federal estate tax at death. The Canada–US Tax Treaty provides some relief, but professional advice is important.
> - **Canadian reporting obligations:** Foreign property holdings over C$100,000 must be reported on your Canadian tax return (T1135).
> - **Provincial health coverage:** Spending more than your province allows in the US (typically 6–7 months total) may affect provincial health coverage.
>
> This is not a deterrent — millions of Canadians own US property successfully. It is a conversation to have with the right professional first.
