# History & Mining — Big Bug District & Henrietta Mine

*Last updated: 2026-04-01 (Poland Mine mill, 20-stamp mill detail, Poland-Walker Tunnel full profile, visible landmarks along Poland Road added)*

---

## Overview

The land that Goswick Ranch occupies sits within one of Arizona's historic gold and silver mining districts. The Big Bug Mining District in the Bradshaw Mountains produced approximately **627,000 ounces of gold between 1867 and 1959**, and the Henrietta Mine — which lies within or immediately adjacent to the subdivision — is among the district's most significant and longest-active mines. Understanding this history is valuable context for the HOA website's "About the Area" content, and the mine's current active status has real-world implications for well water quality and air quality.

Street names inside the subdivision reflect this heritage: **S Miners Pick Rd** and **E Elizabeth Mine Rd** are named for the area's mining history.

---

## Big Bug Mining District

- **Location:** Bradshaw Mountains, Yavapai County, Arizona — directly encompasses the Goswick Ranch area
- **Gold production:** ~627,000 oz (1867–1959); ~42,700 oz from placers
- **Other mines in the district:** Blue Bell, Iron King (between Humboldt and Mayer; $100M total production before 1968 closure), Boggs, French Lily, Little Jessie, Silver Belt
- **Known hazard:** The Big Bug district is a known source of **acid mine drainage and metal leaching** risk to groundwater — one of the reasons arsenic testing of private wells is important at Goswick Ranch (see `01_goswick_ranch_hoa.md`)

## Poland / Poland Junction

**Poland Junction** is a geographic landmark ~1.5 miles southeast of the Henrietta Mine and ~4 miles northwest of Mayer, at the intersection of what was the Prescott-Crown King Railroad spur and the road to the Poland Mine. Elevation: 4,890 ft. Coordinates: 34.452°N, 112.265°W.

### Named For: Davis Robert Poland (1834–1882)

Poland arrived in Yavapai County ~1863 and spent nearly two decades prospecting the Bradshaw Mountains. Key facts:

- **Lynx Creek placer mining** — Poland's first work in the area; significant connection to the Lynx Creek/Lynx Lake drainage that borders Goswick Ranch territory
- **1870** — Co-founded the New Era mine in the Bradshaw district
- **1872** — Discovered the Poland Mine (gold/silver/copper/lead), which became a major producer
- **October 1879** — Sold the Poland Mine to Chicago investors for ~$3.5 million (modern equivalent)
- Served as Judge in Chino Valley (1869) and Arizona Democratic Convention delegate
- Later ranched on the lower Agua Fria; died February 23, 1882, of pneumonia at ~47

### Poland Mine & 20-Stamp Mill

After D.R. Poland sold the mine in 1879, it was redeveloped by **Frank Murphy** (same developer behind the Crown King railway and the Bradshaw Mountain Railroad). In 1902 Murphy built a **20-stamp mill and concentrator** on site.

**Ore processing workflow:**
1. Ore was crushed in the stamp mill and mixed with water into a slurry
2. Slurry piped down via wooden pipe to a filter plant
3. Water extracted; ore loaded onto **narrow-gauge ore cars**
4. Cars hauled by mule through the Poland-Walker Tunnel to the railroad at Poland
5. Rail transport to smelters in Humboldt or Mayer

**Production:**

| Period | Output |
|--------|--------|
| 1907 alone | $130,465 gold + 16,609 oz silver |
| 1900–1912 total | ~$750,000 (primarily silver) |
| Limited runs | 1926, 1930–1931, 1934 |

The mine yielded "druzy quartz with pyrite, sphalerite, and galena" and the Poland vein struck northeast with a steep northwest dip, 800 feet from the south portal of the tunnel. Several thousand feet of drifts plus a 325-foot shaft were developed below tunnel level.

### Poland-Walker Tunnel

The most significant engineering feature on Poland Road — and still **visible** from the road today.

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Length | **8,071 feet** (~1.53 miles; the "1.5-mile" figure used in trail descriptions is accurate) |
| Dimensions | 8 feet tall × 12 feet wide (narrow-gauge ore car clearance) |
| Construction completed | ~1903 |
| Builder | Frank Murphy |
| Purpose | Connect Walker mining district directly to the Poland railroad terminus, eliminating the long mountain wagon route |
| Railroad connection | Ore cars exited the tunnel at Poland onto the Bradshaw Mountain Railroad (Poland branch), then down to the Prescott and Eastern Railroad at Poland Junction |
| Railroad arrival | April 29, 1902; first week of operation moved over $180,000 in gold ore |
| Notable discovery | January 29, 1902 — while blasting a tunnel approach cut, workers uncovered a rich gold/copper vein **400 feet long, 6–12 feet wide** |
| Poland branch abandoned | 1939 |

**Current state of the tunnel:**
- **Poland end (south portal):** Collapsed in 1949; entrance is sealed/caved in — **visible from Poland Road as you approach Five Corners**
- **Walker end (north portal):** Entrance remains open; **water flows out continuously** — the tunnel has been flooded since the collapse
- The interior is not passable

### What's Visible Along Poland Road Today

Driving Poland Road from the gate toward Five Corners, you pass:
1. **Poland Mine mill foundations** — stone and concrete remnants of the 20-stamp mill complex
2. **The sealed south portal of the Poland-Walker Tunnel** — a historic engineering landmark
3. Scattered foundations and tailings from the town of Poland
4. Some summer cabins in the canyon (private property; the area is part-residential today)

These landmarks are passed **before reaching Five Corners** — approximately 5–6 miles up from AZ-69. The site is on private land; observe from the road.

The post office closed in 1913 and the town was largely abandoned, though the tunnel entrance, mill foundations, and a handful of structures remain as one of the Bradshaw Mountains' most accessible mining heritage sites.

### Poland Road — From Junction to Five Corners

Poland Road (CR 58 / NFSR 261) runs ~5.9 miles from Poland Junction up through the Bradshaw Mountains to a high-elevation junction at **~6,926 ft** called **Five Corners** (also "Five Points"). The road follows Big Bug Creek for most of its length, is car-drivable on dirt, and terminates where five forest roads converge on the Big Bug Mesa plateau. It's a sampler of everything the Bradshaws offer: mining history, ponderosa pine forest, OHV terrain, and hiking trailheads. See `05_outdoor_recreation.md` for the full Five Corners / Big Bug Mesa recreation profile.

### Poland Junction (the node)

The junction itself first appears on a 1956 Shell Oil road map. It served as the railroad interchange for ore from both Poland and Walker, feeding the smelting complex at Humboldt and Mayer (the Great Western Smelter, whose 120-ft smokestack still stands in Mayer, was built in 1916 as part of this network).

The 1984–1986 **Poland Junction Mining Corp.** (Mayer, AZ) — listed in the Henrietta Mine timeline below — took its name from this junction, underscoring how tightly the two sites were linked in the district's late-20th-century revival.

### History Timeline

**Pre-1866** — Operating as "Big Bug Mine"
- ~50 ft deep by 1866; producing surface ore
- 1866: among the more active mines in the district

**1883–1884** — "The most prominent mine in the district"
- At peak of early production era

**~1890–1900** — Rediscovery and renaming
- Property apparently renamed for **Henrietta Crossman**, a popular actress and entertainer of the era (exact circumstances unclear; common practice of the period to name mines for well-known figures)

**1915–1919 — Big Ledge Copper Company (Major Redevelopment)**
- The most significant development era
- Construction of a **2,200-ft tunnel** — the main horizontal access drive
- Connected to the **Mayer Smokestack** — a 129.5-ft smelter stack constructed in the town of Mayer in 1917 to carry smelter smoke 8 miles away from the mine workings
- Operations shut down in 1918 as World War I ended and copper prices collapsed

**1923 — Huron Copper Mining Company**
- Reorganized from Big Ledge era
- Ore shipments documented in 1926 and 1930

**1984–1986 — Poland Junction Mining Corp. (Mayer, AZ)**
- Constructed a gravity mill
- Produced approximately **$1.2 million in gold** during this period

**2019** — Henrietta Mine LLC incorporated (Chandler, AZ)

**2020** — New lode claim HM-2 (16.07 acres) staked; 18 active claims total

**April 2022** — ADEQ opened public comment period for **Air Quality Permit No. 92038** for crushing and screening operations at the mine
- ADEQ notice: https://azdeq.gov/notices/comment-period-begins-new-air-quality-permit-crushing-and-screening-henrietta-mine-yavapai

**June 29, 2023** — Arizona State Mine Inspector held a **reclamation public meeting** for Henrietta Mine LLC
- ASMI info: https://asmi.az.gov/meetings/reclamation-public-meetings-operator-henrietta-mine-llc

### Implications for Goswick Ranch

1. **Well water quality:** The mine's proximity is one reason why arsenic and heavy metal testing of private wells is important. Acid mine drainage from the Big Bug district is a documented source of metal leaching (see `01_goswick_ranch_hoa.md` for details).

2. **Air quality:** The 2022 ADEQ air quality permit application for crushing and screening suggests mineral processing activity. Residents should monitor ADEQ notices for updates.

3. **Property values:** The Google Earth aerial overlay shows lower-value adjacent parcels (~$43K–$60K) near the mine tailings vs. the Goswick Ranch premium ($250K–$750K range), likely reflecting proximity to mine operations.

4. **Historical character:** The mining heritage explains the subdivision's street naming (S Miners Pick Rd, E Elizabeth Mine Rd) and is part of the area's authentic Western history. It can be presented as character, not liability — with appropriate well-water awareness.

## Mayer Smokestack — Local Landmark

The **Mayer Smokestack** (constructed 1917) was built at 129.5 feet tall in the town of Mayer itself to carry smelter smoke 8 miles away from the Henrietta Mine's Big Ledge Copper Company operations. It is one of Mayer's most notable historical features, reflecting the scale of early 20th-century mining operations in the Big Bug District.
