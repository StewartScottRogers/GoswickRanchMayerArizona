*Last updated: 2026-04-01*

## Goswick Ranch: Geographic Anchor

**Goswick Ranch is in the Bradshaw Mountains, on Poland Road.**

Poland Road (E Poland Rd / CR 58 / NFSR 261) begins at AZ-69 near the junction known as Poland Junction — approximately 1.5 miles from the Goswick Ranch gate — and climbs ~5.9 miles through the Bradshaw foothills to **Five Corners** (elevation 6,926 ft), the hub of the Prescott National Forest trail and OHV network. Big Bug Creek runs parallel to Poland Road through this corridor.

This positions Goswick Ranch not as a suburb of Mayer, but as a mountain community on a historic road into one of Arizona's great backcountry ranges. Mayer is the nearest town, a mile off AZ-69. The Bradshaws are the back yard.

## Bradshaw Mountains

### Location & Dimensions
- **Location:** Central Arizona; the range runs south from Prescott (~5 miles south of Prescott proper to the northern end; Goswick Ranch is ~27 miles south of Prescott within the range)
- **Orientation:** North–south range, ~40 miles long × ~25 miles wide
- **Eastern border:** Agua Fria River
- **Western border:** Hassayampa River
- **Part of:** Basin and Range region
- **Prescott National Forest** covers much of the range; Bradshaw Ranger District encompasses the area

### Elevation Peaks

| Peak | Elevation |
|------|-----------|
| Mount Union (highest) | 7,979 ft (2,432 m) |
| Mount Davis | 7,897 ft |
| Mount Tritle | 7,793 ft |
| Spruce Mountain | 7,696 ft |
| Towers Mountain | 7,628 ft |

*Goswick Ranch sits in the foothills at approximately 4,400–6,000 ft.*

### Geology
- Primarily **Precambrian granite, gneiss, and schist** — some of the oldest exposed rocks in Arizona, dating to ~1.7–1.8 billion years ago (early Proterozoic)
- **Bradshaw Granite** is the primary intrusive rock; metamorphic belts of Yavapai Precambrian schist interspersed
- Highly mineralized: gold, silver, copper, lead, manganese, tungsten
- The Kwevkapaya Yavapai people mined copper here from approximately 1100–1600 AD
- USGS has published a geologic map specific to Mayer: https://www.usgs.gov/publications/geologic-map-mayer-quadrangle-yavapai-county-arizona

### Soils
The **Cordes series** is documented for northern Yavapai County at 4,000–5,500 ft: coarse-loamy, sandy loam to loamy sand; well-drained; up to 30% gravel; 1–5% slopes in valley positions.

On rocky hillside and upland positions (typical of Goswick Ranch's granite terrain):
- **Very shallow to moderately deep soils** (6 to 24 inches to bedrock)
- Rocky, coarse-textured with significant gravel and cobbles
- Rapid drainage — granite terrain sheds water quickly; high irrigation requirements
- Low water-holding capacity

*Implications: Native plants are best adapted. Septic systems may require alternative designs where bedrock is <36–48 inches. Blasting may be required for foundations.*

## Big Bug Creek

### Geography
- **Location:** Yavapai County, Arizona — in the Bradshaw Mountains
- **Coordinates:** Approx. 34.4364°N, -112.3648°W
- **Watershed HUC:** 15070102
- **Type:** Perennial to seasonal stream; begins just above the Breezy Pines community, flows through Mayer and Spring Valley
- Located in the **Big Bug Mining District** — gold placers discovered in the 1860s, greatest activity in the 1880s
- Named for large beetles that inhabit its banks in June

### Water Quality
- USGS monitoring: Site USGS 09512500

## Native Plant Ecology at 4,400–6,000 ft

Goswick Ranch (~6,000 ft) sits at the upper edge of Arizona's **interior chaparral** zone and the lower fringe of **montane conifer woodland** — an ecologically rich transition zone.

### Dominant Trees

| Species | Notes |
|---------|-------|
| Ponderosa pine (*Pinus ponderosa*) | Arrives above ~5,500 ft; dominant above 6,000 ft |
| Alligator juniper (*Juniperus deppeana*) | Distinctive bark; widespread in transition zone |
| One-seed juniper (*Juniperus monosperma*) | Common at 4,000–5,500 ft |
| Emory oak (*Quercus emoryi*) | Acorns are wildlife food staple |
| Arizona white oak (*Quercus arizonica*) | Chaparral-oak woodland transition |
| Pinyon pine (*Pinus edulis*) | Pinyon-juniper woodland 5,000–7,000 ft |
| Gambel oak (*Quercus gambelii*) | Common above 6,000 ft |

### Dominant Shrubs (Interior Chaparral)
- **Pointleaf manzanita** and **Pringle manzanita** — dominant chaparral species; smooth reddish-brown bark
- **Shrub live oak / turbinella oak** — dense chaparral dominant
- **Birchleaf mountain-mahogany** — nitrogen-fixing; important deer browse
- **Desert ceanothus / Gregg's ceanothus** — nitrogen-fixer; white flower clusters
- Apache plume, skunkbush sumac, cliffrose, Wright silktassel

### Grasses (in openings and post-fire areas)
- Sideoats grama, blue grama, deergrass, sand dropseed, muttongrass, bottlebrush squirreltail

### Cacti & Succulents
- Prickly pear (*Opuntia* spp.) — widespread; definitely present at 6,000 ft
- Cane cholla (*Cylindropuntia imbricata*) — reaches 3,900–7,500 ft; most likely cholla at this elevation
- **Note: Saguaro is NOT present at 6,000 ft.** Saguaro's upper limit is ~4,500–5,000 ft.
- Agave / century plant, banana yucca — present throughout chaparral zone

### Wildflower Season
At ~6,000 ft: **April through May** is the main wildflower window, extending into early June in good rainfall years.
- Key species: lupine (purple/blue, May–June), penstemon (hummingbird magnets), clarkia, sacred datura (large white), globe mallow, owl's clover, desert marigold
- **AZ State Parks Wildflower Bloomdar** (real-time updates): https://azstateparks.com/wildflowers

### Invasive Species Threat: Buffelgrass
Buffelgrass (*Cenchrus ciliaris*) is primarily a lower-elevation threat (southern AZ), not yet widely established in the Bradshaw foothills near Mayer. However, climate warming may push it upslope. Buffelgrass fires reach 871–900°C and fundamentally transform fire-resistant desert into continuously flammable grassland. Purple fountain grass (a related invasive) has been documented in Yavapai County.

## Wildlife at Goswick Ranch Elevation

### Large Mammals

**Mule Deer** (*Odocoileus hemionus*) — dominant deer species in the Bradshaws; most abundant deer in Arizona. Commonly spotted.

**Javelina (Collared Peccary)** — herd animals (up to 20); 35–60 lbs; active dawn/dusk/night; diet includes prickly pear, roots, tubers. Charge when cornered or when dogs are present. Coexistence tips: never feed; secure garbage; remove fallen fruit; electric fencing (single strand 8–10 in above ground) or 4-ft wall to exclude from gardens.

**Mountain Lion (Puma)** — confirmed in the Bradshaws; multiple documented incidents in Prescott area 2023–2024 (one removed near youth OHV in 2024; four removed in spring 2023). If encountered: do NOT run; maintain eye contact; appear large; fight back if attacked; target eyes and nose.

**Black Bear** — confirmed in the Bradshaws (primary habitat 4,000–10,000 ft); Goswick Ranch at ~6,000 ft is squarely within bear range. Shy, most active dawn/dusk; will raid bird feeders, garbage, fruit trees during drought years. Report encounters to AZGFD: 928-692-7700.

**Coyote** — abundant at all elevations; year-round. Bold near subdivisions; attacks on small dogs documented. Keep pets under 25 lbs indoors at night.

**Wild Turkey (Merriam's)** — year-round residents in the Bradshaws; confirmed near Spruce Mountain and throughout the range. Ground feeders; found in ponderosa/oak woodland.

### Reptiles

**Rattlesnakes at 5,000–6,000 ft:**
- **Black-tailed rattlesnake** (*Crotalus molossus*) — primary species at Goswick Ranch elevation; favors mountainous terrain
- **Arizona black rattlesnake** (*Crotalus cerberus*) — documented specifically in the Bradshaw Mountains
- **Western rattlesnake / Prairie rattlesnake** (*Crotalus viridis*) — may be present up to 9,000 ft

Active April–October; can be encountered year-round on warm winter days. Wear boots/long pants in brushy areas; if bitten, go to ER immediately; do NOT cut, suck, or apply tourniquet.

**Gila Monster** — possible but uncommon at 6,000 ft (typical upper range ~5,000 ft, with exceptions to 6,400 ft). Slow-moving; venomous; leave alone if encountered.

### Birds
Arizona has ~550 documented bird species. The chaparral-to-ponderosa transition at Goswick Ranch's elevation offers exceptional diversity:

**Raptors:** Golden Eagle, Red-tailed Hawk, Cooper's Hawk, Northern Goshawk, American Kestrel, Peregrine Falcon

**Hummingbirds:** Anna's Hummingbird (year-round); Black-chinned (spring/summer); **Broad-tailed Hummingbird (primary breeding species in the Bradshaws, April–August)**; Rufous Hummingbird (spring/fall migrant); Calliope Hummingbird (transient)

**Woodpeckers:** Acorn Woodpecker (abundant in oak zones), Hairy Woodpecker, Ladder-backed Woodpecker

**Jays:** Steller's Jay (year-round), Woodhouse's Scrub-Jay, Common Raven

**Songbirds:** Mountain Chickadee, Pygmy Nuthatch, Grace's Warbler (ponderosa pine), Black-throated Gray Warbler, Spotted Towhee, Canyon Towhee, Bewick's Wren, Rock Wren, Greater Roadrunner, Curve-billed Thrasher, Phainopepla, Western Bluebird

**Gamebirds:** Wild Turkey (Merriam's), Scaled Quail

**Owls:** Great Horned Owl, Mexican Spotted Owl, Flammulated Owl, Northern Pygmy-Owl

eBird hotspot resources: https://ebird.org/region/US-AZ-025/hotspots

## Dark Skies

**Bortle Class near Goswick Ranch: 3–4 (Rural Sky)**

- No local streetlights; rural 9-acre minimum lots; no urban core nearby
- Milky Way core dramatically visible spring through early fall
- Zodiacal light sometimes visible; naked-eye limiting magnitude ~6.5 (thousands of stars)
- Prescott Valley proper is Bortle Class 5; surrounding rural/forest areas are Bortle Class 3

**Best viewing periods:**
- October–November: excellent; low humidity; summer Milky Way visible early evening
- January–February: cold but very transparent; Orion, Pleiades
- March–May: galaxy season; good seeing
- **Avoid July–September (monsoon season)** — frequent afternoon/evening clouds

**Resources:**
- Prescott Clear Sky Chart: https://www.cleardarksky.com/c/PrescottAZkey.html
- Light Pollution Map: https://www.lightpollutionmap.info/#zoom=11&lat=34.4&lng=-112.24
- Prescott Astronomy Club (monthly star parties, ~28 mi away): https://prescottastronomyclub.org/

## Monsoon Season at 6,000 ft

**Official Arizona monsoon season: June 15 – September 30.** At Mayer/Goswick Ranch elevation:
- Monsoonal moisture typically first arrives in **early July**
- Afternoon thunderstorms, often rapid with lightning; precipitation can be intense (1–2 inches in 30 minutes)
- Humidity rises dramatically; dust storms possible before storm arrival
- **Flash flood risk is elevated** — especially after any wildfire burn scars upstream
- Post-monsoon (October–June): dry, stable, excellent outdoor weather

*The 2017 Goodwin Fire burn scar resulted in catastrophic Big Bug Creek flooding from just 1–2 inches of monsoon rain in 30 minutes — see `07_emergency_preparedness.md` for full details.*

## Water Quality & Arsenic Risk

**Private well owners: arsenic testing is important.** 43.6% of groundwater samples in Yavapai County exceed the EPA's 10 ppb arsenic limit (NCBI study, 2020). The Big Bug Mining District, which encompasses Goswick Ranch, has documented past mining activity that increases metal leaching risk in fractured bedrock aquifers.

- Private well owners should test every 5 years for arsenic, lead, nitrates, and mining-related metals
- ADHS well water testing resources: https://www.azdhs.gov/wellwater
- ADWR Well Registry (look up specific wells): https://azwatermaps.azwater.gov/wellreg/
- Free testing has been available through ADHS/UA Cooperative Extension grants (check current availability)

## Agua Fria National Monument (Nearby)

- **Managed by:** Bureau of Land Management (BLM)
- **Size:** 71,000 acres
- **Distance from Mayer:** ~15–20 miles south via I-17
- **Admission:** Free; open 24/7 year-round
- **Access:** I-17 at exits 256 (Badger Springs), 259 (Bloody Basin), or 262 (3M Ranch Rd via Cordes Lakes)

**Recreation:** Hiking on unmarked cross-country terrain; petroglyphs (Perry Mesa Archaeological Complex, Pueblo la Plata ~100+ rooms, AD 1250–1450); birding (Audubon Important Bird Area); dispersed camping (14-day limit); no water on site

**Safety:** Summer temps can exceed 110°F; flash flood risk in washes during monsoon; rough terrain; no medical facilities. Best October–April.

BLM contact: Hassayampa Field Office: (602) 867-5400
