## A1. Native Plant Species at 4,400–6,000 ft — Bradshaw Mountain Foothills

### Vegetation Context

Goswick Ranch (~6,000 ft) sits at the upper edge of Arizona's **interior chaparral** zone and the lower fringe of **montane conifer woodland**. Arizona chaparral typically occupies **3,200–5,700 ft elevation** and covers approximately 3.5 million acres statewide. At Goswick Ranch's elevation, the community is exactly in the chaparral-to-ponderosa-pine transition zone — an ecologically rich mixing zone.

Sources:
- [Chaparral | Arizona Wildlife Conservation Strategy (AZGFD)](https://awcs.azgfd.com/habitats/chaparral)
- [Forest and Woodland Overview | AZGFD](https://awcs.azgfd.com/chapter-7-habitat-profiles/forest-and-woodland-overview)

### Dominant Trees at ~5,000–6,500 ft

At this transitional elevation, the **Upper Mogollon Transition zone** (documented 5,100–7,000 ft) features:

| Tree Species | Notes |
|---|---|
| **Ponderosa pine** (*Pinus ponderosa*) | Arrives above ~5,500 ft; becomes dominant above 6,000 ft |
| **Alligator juniper** (*Juniperus deppeana*) | Named for its distinctive bark; widespread in transition zone |
| **One-seed juniper** (*Juniperus monosperma*) | Common at 4,000–5,500 ft |
| **Emory oak** (*Quercus emoryi*) | Acorns are wildlife food staple |
| **Arizona white oak** (*Quercus arizonica*) | Common in chaparral-oak woodland transition |
| **Arizona cypress** (*Cupressus arizonica*) | Rocky drainages and canyon walls |
| **Pinyon pine** (*Pinus edulis*) | Found in pinyon-juniper woodland 5,000–7,000 ft |
| **Gambel oak** (*Quercus gambelii*) | Increasingly common above 6,000 ft |

The Bradshaw Mountains Wikipedia entry confirms: "Climbing the slopes, the desert transitions to grasslands and then thick swaths of chaparral. Other trees include pinyon pine, alligator juniper, and Arizona white oak."

Higher elevations of the Bradshaws (above ~7,000 ft, e.g., Mt. Union at 7,979 ft) add **Douglas fir** and **aspen** — though these are not present at Goswick Ranch's 6,000 ft level (see Fall Color section).

### Dominant Shrubs

Chaparral is characterized by dense, nearly impenetrable thicket. Key shrub species at 5,000–6,000 ft:

**Manzanitas** (genus *Arctostaphylos*) — two species dominate:
- **Pointleaf manzanita** (*Arctostaphylos pungens*) — common in interior chaparral and mid-elevation sky island mountains; sharply pointed leaves, smooth reddish-brown bark; drought-tolerant
- **Pringle manzanita** (*Arctostaphylos pringlei*) — similar but more pubescent (glandular-hairy), blooms earlier; often co-dominates at slightly lower elevations

**Scrub oaks:**
- **Shrub live oak / turbinella oak** (*Quercus turbinella*) — dense chaparral dominant
- **Sonoran scrub oak** (*Quercus turbinella*) — co-dominant
- **Palmer oak** (*Quercus palmeri*)

**Other characteristic shrubs:**
- **Birchleaf mountain-mahogany** (*Cercocarpus betuloides*) — nitrogen-fixing; important deer browse
- **Skunkbush sumac** (*Rhus trilobata*)
- **Sugar sumac / lemonade berry** (*Rhus trilobata*)
- **Desert ceanothus / Gregg's ceanothus** (*Ceanothus greggii*) — nitrogen-fixer; clusters of white flowers
- **Wright silktassel** (*Garrya wrightii*)
- **Hollyleaf buckhorn** (*Rhamnus crocea*)
- **Cliffrose** (*Purshia stansburiana*)
- **Desert olive / New Mexico privet** (*Forestiera pubescens*)
- **Apache plume** (*Fallugia paradoxa*)

- **Sideoats grama** (*Bouteloua curtipendula*) — dominant grass, 5,000–7,000 ft
- **Blue grama** (*Bouteloua gracilis*) — co-dominant, abundant at this elevation
- **Black grama** (*Bouteloua eriopoda*) — lower end of range
- **Sand dropseed** (*Sporobolus cryptandrus*)
- **Deergrass** (*Muhlenbergia rigens*) — clumping, 3–4 ft tall; common in rocky slopes
- **Spike muhly** (*Muhlenbergia wrightii*)
- **Muttongrass** (*Poa fendleriana*)
- **Western wheatgrass** (*Agropyron smithii*)
- **Bottlebrush squirreltail** (*Elymus elymoides*)

## A2. Wildflower Seasons Near Mayer / Bradshaw Foothills

### Bloom Timing by Elevation

Wildflower viewing near Goswick Ranch follows a compressed but spectacular pattern:

| Elevation | Peak Bloom Window | Primary Species |
|---|---|---|
| Lower desert (< 2,500 ft) | Late February – mid-March | Mexican gold poppies, lupine, brittle-bush |
| Mid-elevation (2,500–4,500 ft) | Mid-March – April | Poppies, owl's clover, globe mallow |
| Chaparral (4,500–6,500 ft) | April – May | Penstemon, lupine, clarkia, brodiae |
| High country (6,500–8,000 ft) | May – June | Various perennials, columbine |

For Goswick Ranch specifically (~6,000 ft), the main wildflower window is **April through May**, extending into early June in good rainfall years.

### What Species to Expect

- **Lupine** (*Lupinus* spp.) — purple/blue; blooms May–June at higher elevations; dramatic on roadsides and slopes
- **Penstemon** (*Penstemon* spp.) — red, pink, purple; hummingbird magnets; multiple species at this elevation
- **Clarkia** (farewell-to-spring) — pink/lavender
- **Sacred datura** (*Datura wrightii*) — large white night-blooming trumpets
- **Globe mallow** (*Sphaeralcea ambigua*) — orange; long blooming season
- **Owl's clover** (*Castilleja exserta* / *Orthocarpus purpurascens*) — purple-pink carpets
- **Desert marigold** (*Baileya multiradiata*) — bright yellow; long season
- **Apache plume** (*Fallugia paradoxa*) — white flowers, feathery pink seed heads
- **Wright's buckwheat** (*Eriogonum wrightii*) — white/pink; late summer bloomer
- **Arizona poppy** (*Kallstroemia grandiflora*) — orange; appears after summer monsoon rains

## A3. Invasive Species Threats — Buffelgrass

### What Is Buffelgrass?

**Buffelgrass** (*Cenchrus ciliaris*, formerly *Pennisetum ciliare*) is a perennial bunchgrass from African savannahs, introduced to Arizona in the 1930s–1980s as livestock forage. It is now considered the single greatest ecological threat to the Sonoran Desert ecosystem.

### Why It's Dangerous: The Fire Cycle

Buffelgrass fundamentally transforms fire-resistant desert into a continuously flammable grassland:

- **Fuel loads**: Buffelgrass produces 3–20× more fuel than native annual grasses, and thousands of times more than typical Sonoran Desert fuel loads
- **Fire temperature**: Buffelgrass fires reach **871–900°C** (1,600–1,650°F), and some estimates place stand fires at nearly **1,400°F** — almost 3× hotter than native vegetation fires
- **Continuous fuel bed**: Unlike the naturally sparse native desert, buffelgrass forms a continuous ground cover that carries fire across previously fire-resistant landscapes
- **Grass-fire cycle**: Buffelgrass recovers readily after fire while native plants (saguaro, palo verde, etc.) do not. Each fire increases buffelgrass density and reduces native plants — progressively increasing fire frequency, intensity, and extent
- **Historic fire return interval**: Sonoran Desert historically burned every 250+ years. Buffelgrass infested areas may now burn every 5–10 years.

Sources:
- [Creating Hotter Fires, Fire Ecology Journal / Springer](https://fireecology.springeropen.com/articles/10.4996/fireecology.0902026)
- [Buffelgrass fuel loads, USGS](https://www.usgs.gov/publications/buffelgrass-fuel-loads-saguaro-national-park-arizona-increase-fire-danger-and-threaten)
- [Beat Back Buffelgrass, US Fish & Wildlife Service](https://www.fws.gov/project/beat-back-buffelgrass)
- [NPS Invasive Plant Spotlight: Buffelgrass](https://www.nps.gov/articles/spotlight_buffelgrass.htm)

### Geographic Range — Is It Near Mayer?

Buffelgrass is primarily documented in **southern Arizona** (Pima County, Maricopa County, parts of Pinal County). The primary invasion front runs from Tucson to Phoenix. **Purple fountain grass** (a related invasive) has been documented in Yavapai County, indicating the region is susceptible to invasive grass colonization.

The USDA Forest Service Region 3 (Southwestern Region) — which includes Prescott National Forest — has active invasive species management programs. The Prescott National Forest has partnered with Friends of the Verde River for invasive plant removal. However, **buffelgrass has not been widely documented as established in the Bradshaw foothills near Mayer** as of available data — it remains primarily a lower-elevation threat. The risk of spread upslope with climate warming is documented in research literature.

### Management Recommendations for HOAs

- **Winter pulls**: Manual removal is most effective in winter/early spring before seed set, when soil is moist
- **Herbicide**: Glyphosate applied during active growth (summer); chemical treatment is more effective than mechanical for large patches. Shorter gaps between treatments (< 3 years) yield best results.
- **Community coordination**: Neighbor-by-neighbor removal creates "firebreaks" in the seed bank; isolated removal is less effective if neighboring properties are infested
- **Volunteer programs**: Arizona Sonoran Desert Museum buffelgrass pull events at [desertmuseum.org/buffelgrass](https://www.desertmuseum.org/buffelgrass/)
- **Identification guide**: UA Invasive Plants at [azinvasiveplants.arizona.edu](https://azinvasiveplants.arizona.edu/invasive-plant/buffelgrass)

## A4. Wildlife That Residents Will Encounter

### Javelina (Collared Peccary — *Pecari tajacu*)

**Presence**: Confirmed throughout the Bradshaw Mountains and Prescott area. Roam in herds of up to 20 individuals. Most active at dawn, dusk, and night.

**Behavior**:
- Weigh 35–60 lbs; poor eyesight but excellent hearing and smell
- Diet: prickly pear cactus, roots, tubers, berries, flowers; will dig for grubs in garden soil
- Urban-dwelling javelina in the Prescott area have been studied since 1993 — they adjust home ranges to exploit landscaped yards
- They charge when cornered, protecting young, or when dogs are present; tooth-clacking is a warning display

**Garden damage**: Dig up bulbs, eat ornamentals, uproot plants. Research-based javelina-resistant plant ratings available from [UA Extension (az2098a)](https://extension.arizona.edu/publication/javelina-plant-resistance-ratings). Electric fencing (single strand 8–10 inches above ground) or 4-ft block/chain-link wall with 8–12 inch concrete footer is recommended.

**Coexistence tips**:
- Never feed; secure garbage; remove fallen fruit
- Scare off with loud noise, small rocks thrown in their direction, or diluted ammonia spray (1:9 ratio)
- Keep dogs leashed and close; pick up small pets if javelina approach

Sources:
- [Living with Javelina, AZGFD](https://www.azgfd.com/wildlife-conservation/living-with-wildlife/living-with-javelina/)
- [Javelina-Resistant Plants, UA Extension](https://extension.arizona.edu/publication/javelina-plant-resistance-ratings)
- [Urban javelina study, Prescott area](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1023/A:1009581632050)

### Mountain Lion (Puma — *Puma concolor*)

**Presence**: Confirmed in the Bradshaw Mountains. Active in Prescott urban fringe — multiple documented incidents 2023–2024:
- 2024: AZGFD lethally removed one lion from an eastern Prescott neighborhood after it approached within 5 feet of a youth on an OHV
- Spring 2023: Four mountain lions removed from Prescott area; one attacked a dog
- Range warning: Lion sightings were increasing in northern Arizona per 2023 park ranger reports

**Behavior**: Solitary ambush hunters; stand 2.0–2.7 ft tall; can jump 18 ft vertically and 40 ft horizontally. Most active dawn and dusk. Attacks on humans are rare but not unheard of.

**Encounter protocol**:
1. Do NOT run — this triggers prey response
2. Maintain eye contact; do not crouch or turn your back
3. Make yourself appear large — raise arms, open jacket, speak loudly and firmly
4. Fight back if attacked — target eyes and nose
5. Report all sightings to AZGFD

### Black Bear (*Ursus americanus*)

**Presence**: Confirmed in the Bradshaw Mountains. Primary habitat is 4,000–10,000 ft in forested and semi-forested areas. Goswick Ranch's elevation (~6,000 ft) is squarely within bear range.

**Behavior**:
- Generally shy and solitary; most active dawn and dusk
- Omnivores: berries, acorns, insects, carrion; will raid bird feeders, garbage, and fruit trees
- Hibernate November–March in dens; cubs born in January
- During drought years, bears may travel long distances (home ranges 5–50 sq miles) to find food — and may enter subdivisions
- Confirmed in Prescott Valley: May 2024 sighting prompted public warning from police

**Coexistence tips**:
- Secure garbage in bear-proof containers
- Remove bird feeders during active season (April–October)
- Do not leave pet food outside
- Report encounters to AZGFD: 928-692-7700

Sources:

### Coyote (*Canis latrans*)

**Presence**: Abundant throughout the Bradshaws and all elevations. Year-round residents.

**Behavior**:
- Primarily prey on rabbits, rodents, birds, snakes; will eat fruit and carrion
- Most active at dusk and dawn; increasingly bold in suburban/rural subdivisions
- Bold coyotes have attacked small dogs on retractable leashes
- Pack animals but usually hunt alone

**Pet safety**:
- Keep small pets (under 25 lbs) indoors at night
- Never leave pets unattended outside at dawn/dusk
- Walk on short, fixed-length leash; pick up small pets if coyotes approach
- Remove attractants: pet food, fallen fruit, unsecured garbage

**Hazing techniques**: Maintain coyote "fear of humans" through aggressive body language — yell, wave arms, throw sticks. Motion-activated lights and sprinklers help.

### Rattlesnakes

**Species at 5,000–6,000 ft in the Bradshaws**:

| Species | Elevation Range | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Black-tailed rattlesnake** (*Crotalus molossus*) | 1,000–12,300 ft (300–3,750 m) | **Primary species at Goswick Ranch elevation**; favors higher mountainous terrain; named for black tail |
| **Arizona black rattlesnake** (*Crotalus cerberus*) | Bradshaw Mountains confirmed habitat | Smaller, darker; documented in Bradshaw Mountains specifically |
| **Western rattlesnake / Prairie rattlesnake** (*Crotalus viridis*) | Up to 9,000 ft | May be present |
| **Western diamondback** (*Crotalus atrox*) | Sea level to 6,500 ft | Possible at lower end of property; more common below 5,000 ft |

Note: The Arizona Black Rattlesnake (*Crotalus cerberus*) has been photographed in the Bradshaw Mountains specifically. The Black-tailed Rattlesnake is the species most associated with higher elevation mountainous terrain like Goswick Ranch.

**Season**: Most active April–October; can be encountered year-round on warm winter days at lower elevations. Dawn and dusk are peak activity times.

**Safety**:
- Watch where you step and place hands when hiking
- Wear boots and long pants in brushy areas
- Never handle; give wide berth (6 feet)
- Teach children to freeze and call an adult if they see a snake
- If bitten: stay calm, immobilize limb, go to emergency room immediately; do NOT cut, suck, or apply tourniquet

### Gila Monster (*Heloderma suspectum*)

**Presence at 6,000 ft**: Rarely, but possible. Typical upper range is ~5,000 ft in Arizona, with exceptional records at 5,800 ft in the Mule Mountains (AZ) and up to 6,400 ft in New Mexico. At Goswick Ranch's 6,000 ft elevation, encounters are possible but uncommon.

**Behavior**: Slow-moving, venomous (only venomous lizard in the U.S.); spends 95% of life underground. Active April–October. Bites if provoked; venom causes intense pain but is rarely fatal to healthy adults. Leave alone if encountered.

Sources:
- [Gila Monster, Tucson Herp Society](https://tucsonherpsociety.org/amphibians-reptiles/lizards/gila-monster/)

### White-tailed Deer vs. Mule Deer

At Goswick Ranch's elevation (~6,000 ft) in the central Bradshaws:

**Mule deer** (*Odocoileus hemionus*) is the **dominant deer species** in the Bradshaw Mountains and throughout the Prescott National Forest. They are commonly spotted and frequently referenced in all Bradshaw Mountains wildlife lists. Mule deer are Arizona's most abundant deer overall.

**Coues' white-tailed deer** (*Odocoileus virginianus couesi*) is a smaller subspecies found in Arizona but more concentrated in the southeast "sky island" mountains (Huachuca, Chiricahua, Santa Rita). In the Bradshaw Mountains area (central AZ, Yavapai County), mule deer predominate, with white-tails rare or absent.

### Wild Turkey (*Meleagris gallopavo* — Merriam's subspecies)

**Presence**: **Year-round residents** in the Bradshaw Mountains. Multiple sources confirm turkeys near Spruce Mountain (just east of Senator Highway in the Bradshaws) and throughout the range.

**Species**: **Merriam's turkey** (*M. g. merriami*) — the subspecies of Arizona's central highlands. Found at elevations 3,500–10,000 ft, primarily in ponderosa pine habitat with oak understory. Best populations north of the Gila River — Prescott area is prime territory.

**Habitat**: Dense manzanita thickets for shelter; ponderosa pine/oak woodland for foraging. Turkeys are ground feeders, eating acorns, seeds, berries, and insects.

## A6. Dark Sky Details

### Bortle Class / Sky Quality

Based on available data for the Prescott/Mayer area:

- **Prescott Valley proper**: Bortle Class 5 (suburban sky)
- **Surrounding rural areas / Prescott National Forest**: **Bortle Class 3** (rural sky — "excellent contrast for the Milky Way and bright star clusters")

## A7. Soil Types and Geology

### Bedrock Geology

The Bradshaw Mountains are underlain by **Precambrian granite, gneiss, and schist** — some of the oldest exposed rocks in Arizona, dating to approximately **1.7–1.8 billion years ago** (early Proterozoic). Specifically:

- **Bradshaw Granite** — the primary intrusive rock; a coarse-grained granite
- **Yavapai Precambrian schist** — metamorphic rock in intervening belts
- **Gabbro, granodiorite, and granite plutons** — with supracrustal belts of metavolcanic and metasedimentary schist
- Metamorphism reached greenschist to amphibolite facies near plutonic intrusions
- The Verde Fault (near the range's eastern margin) has displacements up to 1,770 feet

USGS has published a geologic map specific to Mayer:
- [Geologic map of the Mayer quadrangle, Yavapai County, Arizona, USGS](https://www.usgs.gov/publications/geologic-map-mayer-quadrangle-yavapai-county-arizona)

### Soil Characteristics

Based on regional soil survey data (NRCS/SSURGO for Yavapai County):

The **Cordes series** is documented for northern Yavapai County in swales and alluvial fans at **4,000–5,500 ft elevation**, formed from weathered granite, schist, and basalt. Characteristics:
- Coarse-loamy, sandy loam to loamy sand
- Well-drained; moderately rapid permeability
- Up to 30% gravel content
- 1–5% slopes in valley positions

For the rocky hillside and upland positions typical of a subdivision like Goswick Ranch on granite bedrock terrain, expect:
- **Very shallow to moderately deep soils** (6 inches to 24 inches to bedrock) — typical of granite-derived chaparral soils
- **Rocky, coarse-textured** with significant gravel and cobbles throughout the profile
- **Rapid drainage** — granite terrain sheds water quickly; irrigation requirements are high
- **Low water-holding capacity** — plants stressed during drought unless deep-rooted

### Implications

**Gardening**:
- Native plants are best adapted; non-natives will struggle without supplemental irrigation
- Soil pH tends toward slightly acidic to neutral on granitic soils
- Organic matter content typically low; amend with compost for vegetable gardens
- Raised beds are effective on shallow-soil sites

**Septic systems**:
- Shallow bedrock is a common septic challenge in the Bradshaw foothills
- Many lots require alternative (mound or drip-field) septic designs when bedrock is < 36–48 inches
- Perc test results vary dramatically even on adjacent lots depending on bedrock depth
- Yavapai County Health Department governs septic permits

**Construction**:
- Blasting may be required for foundations and utilities in granite
- Shallow bedrock can complicate well drilling (though bedrock wells in granite can be excellent producers)
- Erosion risk during construction is high on granitic soils — disturbed granitic soil (DG — decomposed granite) is highly erodible

## A8. Monsoon Season at 6,000 ft — The Experience

### Season Timing

Arizona's official monsoon season: **June 15 – September 30**. In the Bradshaw Mountains near Prescott/Mayer:
- Monsoonal moisture typically first reaches the area in **early July**
- Peak activity: **mid-July through mid-August**
- Season ends: **late September** — followed almost immediately by crystal-clear fall skies

### Storm Character at Elevation

The Bradshaws are an **orographic trigger** for monsoon storms. Documented patterns:

> "Around Prescott, storms form over the Black Hills to the east and the **Bradshaws to the south**. Thunderstorms tend to trigger over the higher terrain first due to thermal circulations that flow up the slopes and converge at higher peaks."

At 6,000 ft, the monsoon experience is more intense than the valley floor:
- **Earlier triggering**: High terrain heats faster; storm buildup begins by **early to mid-afternoon** (2–4 PM typically), vs. 4–6 PM in Phoenix
- **More direct lightning exposure**: Elevated terrain receives more lightning strikes; do not be outside on ridgelines or exposed slopes during storm development
- **Dramatic visual shows**: Lightning over the Bradshaws is well-documented in photography; storms building over adjacent peaks are visible from Goswick Ranch
- **Intense brief rainfall**: Monsoon cells dump 1–3 inches in 20–45 minutes; total annual precipitation at this elevation is approximately 18–25 inches, with nearly half falling June–September

### Lightning Frequency

Arizona sees over **500,000 lightning strikes per monsoon season**. The Bradshaw Mountains and central Arizona highlands are in a region of elevated lightning density. All Arizona lightning fatalities in recent decades occurred outdoors — hiking, camping, or working outside.

**Safety rules at elevation**:
- Go inside permanent structures or vehicles by 2 PM on summer days if clouds are building
- Never shelter under lone trees or rock overhangs
- Stay off ridgelines and elevated terrain during storm approach
- Count seconds between flash and thunder: 5 sec = 1 mile distance; retreat at under 30 seconds (6 miles)

### Flash Flood Risk on 9-Acre Lots

Goswick Ranch's 9-acre lots likely include one or more **dry washes** (arroyos). These are intermittent stream channels that are completely dry most of the year but can experience violent flash flooding during monsoon:

- **Response time**: A wash can go from dry to 3–6 feet of rushing water in 5–10 minutes, triggered by a storm miles upstream
- **Never cross** a flowing wash, even if the water looks shallow
- **Do not camp** in or near dry washes June–September
- Determine which washes cross or border your lot before monsoon season; know which way water flows

### GMRS (General Mobile Radio Service)

**GMRS is ideal for Goswick Ranch community communication** — it requires no technical exam, is relatively inexpensive, and provides reliable local communication.

**License details**:
- FCC license: **$35 for 10 years** (no exam required; applicant must be 18+)
- One license covers **entire immediate family** (spouse, children, parents, siblings, etc.) and extended family

**GMRS frequencies**: Channels around **462 MHz and 467 MHz**

**For community use**:
- Any licensed individual can use GMRS repeaters
- GMRS repeaters dramatically extend range — a standard handheld radio (3–5 mile range) can reach 20+ miles through a mountaintop repeater
- Multiple community members can hold their own licenses and designate shared community channels

**GMRS Repeaters Near Prescott/Mayer**:

1. Each household gets an FCC GMRS license ($35 / 10 years at fcc.gov)
2. Purchase GMRS radios — mid-range handhelds ($50–$150 each; popular brands: Midland, Motorola, BTECH)
3. Designate a primary community channel and backup channel
4. Program the Mt. Union repeater (462.600 / CTCSS 167.9) into all radios for extended-range communication
5. Establish a weekly or monthly radio check protocol
6. Post channel list and basic operating instructions on HOA website

## B5. What3Words for Rural Addressing

### What Is What3Words?

What3words (what3words.com) divides the entire world into **3-meter × 3-meter squares** (approximately 10 feet × 10 feet), each with a unique 3-word address (e.g., `///chair.smoke.rivers`). There are no coordinates to remember — just three common words.

**Coverage at Goswick Ranch**: Yes, what3words covers the entire globe including all of rural Arizona. Every square foot of Goswick Ranch has a what3words address. The **free app works offline** — even without cell service, residents can find their 3-word address using GPS. This is significant for an area with spotty cellular coverage.

### How It Works for 911

1. Caller opens what3words app, sees their 3-word address
2. Caller tells dispatcher the 3-word address verbally or texts it
3. Dispatcher enters it in their system for precise GPS coordinates

### Is What3Words Integrated with 911 in Yavapai County?

**Status as of research date (March 2026)**:

- Arizona completed statewide **Next Generation 911 (NG911)** rollout as of **September 29, 2025** — all 79 PSAPs (Public Safety Answering Points) are now on NG911, including Yavapai County
- Arizona's NG911 system includes advanced caller location capabilities (pinpointing callers within 3 feet via cell phone GPS)
- **What3words-specific integration**: No documentation found confirming Yavapai County has formally adopted what3words in its dispatch system. What3words is integrated in 600+ PSAPs across 28 states nationally, but Arizona PSAP adoption is not confirmed.

### What This Means for Goswick Ranch Residents

Even **without formal 911 integration**, what3words is useful:
- Use it to **communicate exact location** to friends, contractors, delivery drivers, and emergency services
- When calling 911, you can say: "I'm at what3words address [three words] — do you support that?" and if not, provide cross-streets or GPS coordinates as backup
- The app shows GPS coordinates alongside the 3-word address

## SUMMARY OF KEY FINDINGS

### Topic A Highlights
1. **Goswick Ranch sits at the top of the chaparral zone** — the manzanita-scrub oak dominated interior chaparral transitions to ponderosa pine at this exact elevation. This is an ecologically rich and visually distinctive landscape.
2. **No saguaros** at this elevation. Prickly pear yes; cane cholla possible; agave and yucca yes.
3. **Wildflowers peak April–May** at this elevation — penstemon, lupine, globe mallow, sacred datura.
4. **Buffelgrass** is primarily a lower-elevation threat (Tucson–Phoenix corridor) and has not been widely documented in the Bradshaw foothills, but the risk of northward expansion with warming climate is real.
5. **Black bears and mountain lions are genuinely present** at Goswick Ranch's elevation — not theoretical. Active AZGFD incidents in Prescott suburbs confirm proximity. Javelina are abundant.
6. **Dark skies are exceptional** — likely Bortle Class 3, possibly approaching Bortle 2 away from the Prescott light dome. SQM 21.37 is the regional benchmark (very dark).
7. **The monsoon is dramatic at elevation** — storms form over the Bradshaws. Flash flood risk in dry washes is real. Best astronomy October–June.
8. **Mule deer** are the deer species; wild turkeys are year-round residents (Merriam's subspecies).
9. **Broad-tailed hummingbirds** breed in the Bradshaws; Anna's are year-round. Up to 11 species recorded in the Prescott area.

### Topic B Highlights
1. **Gate systems**: LiftMaster + Knox Box is the practical combination for a rural HOA. DoorKing or CellGate add cellular options. Budget approximately $3,000–$6,000 installed for a complete system with solar backup.
2. **Wildfire cameras**: APS AI cameras on **Mount Francis and Mount Union** provide direct coverage of the Bradshaw Mountains including the Goswick Ranch area. Detection range is 10 miles. Alerts go to USFS and county emergency management.
3. **HAM radio**: The **Mt. Union repeater (147.260 MHz / CTCSS 103.5)** — located on the highest peak in the Bradshaws — provides excellent Yavapai County coverage. K7YCA (Yavapai ARES) holds nets Monday evenings.
4. **GMRS**: The **Mt. Union GMRS repeater (462.600 MHz output / CTCSS 167.9)** also covers the area. $35 GMRS license per family for 10 years; no exam required. Ideal for Goswick Ranch community emergency network.
5. **HOA app**: For a 15-home HOA, start with **WhatsApp or GroupMe** (free, immediate) and graduate to **HOA Messenger** (free tier) for document management and broadcast messaging.
6. **What3words**: Works offline; covers all of Goswick Ranch; Arizona NG911 is statewide as of late 2025, but what3words-specific dispatch integration in Yavapai County is unconfirmed — residents can still use it to communicate location precisely.
