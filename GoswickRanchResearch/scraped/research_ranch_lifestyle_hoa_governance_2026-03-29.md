#### ADU (Accessory Dwelling Unit) Rules

As of November 19, 2025, Yavapai County adopted new ADU standards implementing recent Arizona state law:

- **One attached and one detached ADU** are permitted on single-family lots (including RCU-2A)
- **Maximum size: 1,000 sq ft** for standard parcels (the Board rejected a 1,500 sq ft option)
- **Setbacks:** 5 feet for side and rear yards
- On **parcels of 1 acre or more**, an additional detached ADU with larger size options may be permitted subject to county standards and environmental review
- Building permits and environmental review (including septic/water capacity adequacy) remain required
- ADUs are exempt from additional parking requirements under state law, but replacement parking is required if construction removes existing spaces
- **Private CC&Rs remain enforceable** — an HOA can still prohibit or regulate ADUs under its governing documents even if county allows them

Source: https://citizenportal.ai/articles/7306141/Yavapai-County/Arizona/Yavapai-County-adopts-accessorydwelling-rules-to-implement-state-law-adds-sewer-adequacy-and-a-1000sqft-cap

#### Short-Term Rentals

- Arizona state law (effective January 1, 2017) allows short-term rental of permitted habitable structures in unincorporated areas.
- Yavapai County **cannot ban STRs outright** under state preemption law (ARS § 11-269.17), but **may regulate** noise, nuisance, welfare, and property maintenance issues, applied in the same manner as other residential properties.
- **Bed & Breakfast Homestay** definition: owner-occupied residence with up to 3 guest units serving breakfast to guests — requires owner occupancy.
- STRs in unincorporated Yavapai County **do NOT allow events** such as weddings, parties, specialized retreats, or commercial activities.
- The most recent ordinance update (effective July 18, 2025) governs.
- **HOA CC&Rs may additionally restrict STRs** beyond what county allows.

Source: https://www.yavapaiaz.gov/Development-and-Permits/Codes-Ordinances/Ordinances-and-Regulations

### 2. Arizona Agricultural Property Tax (AG Classification)

**Key source:** Arizona DOR Agricultural Property Manual (2022): https://azdor.gov/sites/default/files/2023-03/PROPERTY_AgriculturalPropertyManual.pdf

#### What qualifies a property for agricultural classification?

- The **primary use** of the land must be agricultural
- There must be a **reasonable expectation of operating profit**, exclusive of land cost
- The property must have been **in active production for at least 3 of the last 5 years** using generally accepted agricultural practices

#### Minimum acreage requirements by operation type

| Operation Type | Minimum Acreage |
|---|---|
| Cropland | 20 gross acres (aggregate) |
| Permanent crops (orchards) | 10 gross acres |
| Grazing land | 40 animal units carrying capacity (not acreage per se) |
| High-density operations (dairies, feedlots, wholesale nurseries) | No minimum acreage |
| Equine operations (training or rescue) | No minimum animal unit requirement; must show reasonable expectation of profit |

Application deadline: **September 30** each year for the following tax year. Form: DOR Form 82916 (Agricultural Land Use Application).

Source: https://legalclarity.org/how-to-get-an-arizona-farm-tax-exemption/
Source: https://www.gilacountyaz.gov/government/assessor/agriculture.php

#### Can a Goswick Ranch 9-acre property qualify for AG classification?

- **Standard cropland (20 ac) and orchard (10 ac) minimums would disqualify** a 9-acre lot on their own.
- **Exception:** High-density and equine operations have no minimum acreage — a property with a genuine commercial equine training, breeding, or rescue operation could potentially qualify, but **must demonstrate commercial intent and reasonable profit expectation**.
- Hobby farms, personal gardens, private orchards, and small-scale personal operations **do not qualify**.
- "A rancher who breeds and raises horses is considered to be engaging in an agricultural activity, and therefore, the horses are exempt from property taxation." (Yavapai County Assessor)
- **The Yavapai County Assessor conducts a physical inspection** as part of the review process.

Source: https://yavapaiaz.gov/assessor/agricultural
Source: https://www.yavapaiaz.gov/Mapping-and-Properties/Assessors-Office

#### Tax rate difference: Agricultural (Class 2) vs. Residential (Class 3/4)

Arizona property is taxed as: assessed value × tax rate. The assessment ratio (applied to full cash value to determine assessed value) differs by class:

| Class | Type | Assessment Ratio |
|---|---|---|
| Class 2 | Agricultural land and vacant land | 15% |
| Class 3 | Owner-occupied residential (primary residence) | 10% |
| Class 4 | Non-primary residential / rental | 10% |

**Counterintuitive result:** Agricultural Class 2 actually has a **higher** assessment ratio (15%) than residential Class 3 (10%). This means agricultural classification, by itself, does not automatically lower taxes — it depends on whether the property's **limited cash value** for agricultural use is significantly lower than its residential limited cash value. For rural ranch land being used purely for agriculture, the productive value (income capitalization method) may be far lower than market value, producing significant tax savings. However, for a 9-acre parcel with a home on it that is zoned residential, the improvement value (house) is still assessed separately at residential rates.

**Bottom line:** Agricultural classification primarily benefits large working ranches. A 9-acre hobby horse property is unlikely to achieve significant savings; a genuine commercial equine operation *might* qualify and could see reduced land valuation, but the analysis is property-specific.

Source: https://azdor.gov/sites/default/files/2023-03/PROPERTY_PropertyClassification.pdf
Source: https://www.yavapaiaz.gov/files/sharedassets/public/v/1/mapping-and-properties/documents/assessment_and_valuation_pamphlet.pdf

### 3. Open Range Law in Arizona

#### Summary

Arizona is a **"fence out" (open range) state**. The responsibility for keeping livestock off private property **falls on the property owner**, not the livestock owner. Property owners who do not want livestock on their land must build a legally compliant fence ("lawful fence").

**A lawful fence in Arizona (ARS § 3-1426) requires:**
- Posts spaced no more than 30 feet apart, firmly placed in the ground
- At least 4 strands of barbed wire, tightly stretched
- Wire heights: top wire at 50 inches; others at 38, 28, and 18 inches above ground

#### Liability for livestock on roads — critical point

Under Arizona open range law:
> **If you hit and kill or injure livestock on the open range, you (the driver) are liable to the livestock owner, not the reverse.**

The livestock owner generally bears no liability for a vehicle collision in open range territory unless the livestock owner was negligent (e.g., leaving a gate open near a highway). This is a well-established principle of Arizona law.

#### Is Yavapai County open range?

Yavapai County is **open range** outside of incorporated municipalities and any designated no-fence districts. No specific no-fence district covering the Mayer/Poland Road/SR 69 corridor was identified in research.

- **No-fence districts** can be created by petition to the County Board of Supervisors under ARS § 3-1421. Requirements: 75% of acreage in an irrigation district of 20,000+ acres, OR contiguous body of 1,000+ acres adjoining an incorporated city of 30,000+ people where majority of taxpayers petition.
- These thresholds are unlikely to be met in the rural Mayer area, so it almost certainly remains open range.

#### Implications for Goswick Ranch residents

- Livestock from neighboring ranches can legally roam onto unfenced Goswick Ranch properties.
- A resident who hits cattle on E Poland Rd or SR 69 may be **liable to the cattle owner**, not vice versa.
- Residents who want to exclude livestock must build a lawful fence at their own expense.
- This is a real consideration along SR 69 and Poland Road, which pass through open range territory.

### 7. Arizona HOA Statutes — Key Provisions (ARS Title 33, Chapter 16)

Arizona's **Planned Communities Act** (A.R.S. §§ 33-1801 through 33-1821) governs planned community HOAs like Goswick Ranch. Key provisions:

#### Record-Keeping Requirements (ARS § 33-1805)

- Association must make **"financial and other records...reasonably available for examination"** by any member or their designated representative
- HOA has **10 business days** to fulfill a request to examine records
- HOA has **10 business days** to provide copies when requested
- **Copying fee cap:** No more than **$0.15 per page**
- Members **cannot be charged** for merely reviewing materials
- **Exceptions** (may be withheld): attorney-client communications, pending litigation details, closed board meeting minutes, personal/health information about individuals, employee performance records
- **Duration of retention:** The statute does not specify a minimum retention period (HOA's governing documents or best practice guides should address this)
- **Meeting recordings** (2025 update): Board meeting recordings must be retained for **at least 6 months** and made available to any member on request (unedited copy)

Source: https://www.azleg.gov/ars/33/01805.htm (fetched directly)

#### Open Meeting Requirements (ARS § 33-1804)

- **All meetings** (member association and board of directors) are open to all members and designated representatives
- Members have the right to **attend and speak** at appropriate times during deliberations
- **Board meetings (post-declarant control):** Notice must be given **at least 48 hours in advance** via newsletter, conspicuous posting, or any other reasonable means
- **Member meetings:** Notice must be provided **10–50 days beforehand** via mail, hand delivery, or electronic means, and must include date, time, location, and purposes
- **Agendas** must be distributed via multiple channels (mail, website, email, or posting)
- **Member speaking right:** Board shall allow a member to speak **once after board discussion but before formal action** on each agenda item
- **Recording:** Attendees may audio or video record open session portions **without advance permission**
- **Closed sessions permitted only for:** legal advice, pending litigation, personal/financial member information, employee performance matters, or member appeal discussions (unless member requests open session)

Source: https://www.azleg.gov/ars/33/01804.htm (fetched directly)

#### Assessment Limitations (ARS § 33-1803)

- Regular assessments **cannot increase by more than 20%** year-over-year without approval of a **majority of members**
- Community documents may impose stricter limits
- Late payments: deemed late if unpaid **15+ days** after due date (unless documents provide longer period)
- **Late fee cap:** Greater of $15 or 10% of unpaid assessment
- Payments applied **first to principal, then to interest**

Source: https://www.azleg.gov/ars/33/01803.htm (fetched directly)

#### Election Rules (ARS § 33-1812)

- **Proxy voting is PROHIBITED** after termination of declarant control ("votes allocated to a unit may not be cast pursuant to a proxy")
- Exception for timeshare communities only
- **Absentee ballots are required** — associations must provide voting by absentee ballot in addition to in-person voting; email and fax delivery may also be offered
- Absentee ballots must: set forth each proposed action, provide yes/no opportunity, be valid for one specified election only (auto-expire after completion)
- Members must receive ballots **at least 7 days before deadline**
- Completed ballots require voter name, address, and signature (unless secret ballot rules apply)
- **Record retention:** Ballots and related materials (envelopes, sign-in sheets) must be retained in electronic or paper format for **at least 1 year** after the election
- **Note:** Arizona's statute does NOT explicitly require an independent inspector of elections (unlike California)

Source: https://www.azleg.gov/ars/33/01812.htm (fetched directly)

#### Resale Disclosure Requirements (ARS § 33-1806)

The association must:
- Deliver required documents **within 10 days** of receiving written notice of a pending sale
- Provide purchaser with all of the following:
  1. Governing documents (bylaws, rules, declaration)
  2. Dated statement: contact info, current assessments owed, insurance details, reserve fund totals, violations of declaration, pending litigation
  3. Current operating budget
  4. Most recent annual financial report (or summary if over 10 pages)
  5. Most recent reserve study (if one exists)
  6. Pending lawsuit summary (excluding member collection actions)
  7. Acknowledgment form for purchaser signature

**Fee caps:**
- Base disclosure fee: **maximum $400 aggregate**
- Rush fee (72-hour turnaround): **up to $100 additional**
- Update fee (if 30+ days since original statement): **up to $50 additional**
- Fees collected **only at escrow closing**, charged once per transaction
- Penalty for overcharging: up to **$1,200 civil penalty**

Source: https://www.azleg.gov/ars/33/01806.htm (fetched directly)

#### 2025 Arizona HOA Legislative Updates (effective September 26, 2025)

- **Meeting recordings:** Must be kept at least 6 months and provided to members on request (unedited)
- **Foreclosure threshold:** Associations cannot file judicial lien foreclosure until owner is **delinquent 18 months OR owes $10,000+**, whichever comes first
- **Political signs:** Definition expanded to include flags — limits HOA's ability to restrict these
- **ADUs:** Counties/municipalities must allow at least one attached and one detached ADU on single-family lots (private CC&Rs still enforceable)
- **Small claims limit:** Increased from $3,500 to $5,000

Source: https://gshoalaw.com/az-hoa-legislative-update-2025/

### 8. Self-Managed HOA Financial Management

#### Two-account structure (best practice)

Every HOA, regardless of size, should maintain at minimum:
1. **Operating account** (checking) — day-to-day expenses, assessment collection, vendor payments
2. **Reserve account** (savings or money market) — dedicated fund for major repairs and replacements; should not be mingled with operating funds

#### Reserve fund funding levels

- Industry best practice: **70–100% funded** relative to projected reserve needs
- Common rule of thumb: allocate **15–40% of annual budget** to reserves
- For a small community (10–20 homes), this percentage may need to be higher because the cost of major repairs falls on a smaller owner base
- Reserve fund should use **business savings or money market accounts** rather than checking, to earn interest and signal separation

#### Is a reserve study required in Arizona?

**No — Arizona statutes do NOT require planned communities to commission a reserve study.** However:
- Planned communities with **50 or more lots** must disclose the amount in reserves and provide a copy of the most recent reserve study (if any) to purchasers
- For communities **under 50 lots** (such as Goswick Ranch), no reserve study disclosure is required at resale
- Despite no legal mandate, reserve studies are strongly recommended as best practice, especially for small communities
- Recommended update cycle: **full on-site study every 3 years**, with lighter annual reviews in between

**Arizona state law (enacted 2005) DOES require:**
- HOA boards must complete an **annual financial audit, review, or compilation** within 180 days of fiscal year-end
- Results must be made available to members within 30 days

Sources:
- https://www.propfusion.com/law-guide/arizona-reserve-study-requirements
- https://facilitiesadvisors.com/arizona-reserve-laws
- https://hoatax.com/arizona-hoa-tax-return-filing-requirements/

#### Internal controls for small HOAs

- Assign a dedicated Treasurer
- Reserve withdrawals should require **board approval and dual signatures**
- Avoid giving a single person unrestricted access to reserve funds
- Use HOA accounting software (even free options) to maintain consistent records
- Transparency: share reserve fund reports at annual meetings and with any requesting member

### 9. HOA Insurance Requirements

#### Types of coverage for an Arizona HOA

| Coverage Type | Legal Requirement | Recommendation |
|---|---|---|
| Commercial General Liability (CGL) | Required by Planned Communities Act for common areas | Essential — covers bodily injury/property damage in common areas |
| Directors & Officers (D&O) | **Not legally required** by AZ statute — optional | Strongly recommended; protects board members personally |
| Property/Casualty | Required for physical common structures/amenities | Required if association owns/maintains physical property |
| Umbrella/Excess Liability | Not required | Recommended for extra protection above CGL limits |

**Important note for Goswick Ranch:** If the HOA owns minimal common area (a gate, an entry sign, perhaps a shared road), the required CGL coverage is simpler and less expensive than a community with pools, clubhouses, and parks. D&O remains recommended because **board members can be personally sued** even in a small community.

#### Typical costs for a small (10–20 home) rural HOA

- Standard **$1M–$2M CGL policy:** approximately **$57–$79/month** ($684–$948/year)
- Full HOA insurance package (CGL + D&O): ranges widely, but the **$2,000–$5,000/year** range is typical for a small rural community with minimal common areas
- Some sources cite $50–$100/month for a basic package
- A rural community with no pool, clubhouse, or playground will be toward the **lower end** of these ranges

### 10. HOA Tax Filing

#### Federal — IRS Form 1120-H

- A homeowners association files **Form 1120-H** to elect treatment as a homeowners association under IRC § 528
- **There is no income threshold that exempts a small HOA from this election** — the election itself is the filing
- **Qualification tests:**
  - At least **60% of income** must come from member dues, fees, and assessments
  - At least **90% of expenses** must go toward acquiring, building, managing, or maintaining association property
  - No part of net earnings may benefit any individual
- **Tax rate:** 30% flat rate on "non-exempt function income" (any income NOT from member assessments — e.g., interest income, rental of common areas to non-members)
- Most small HOAs with only member assessment income pay **zero federal tax**, but still should file the 1120-H to claim exempt status and put the election on record
- **2025 change:** Tax year 2025 is the first year e-filing is available for Form 1120-H; HOAs submitting 10+ returns must e-file
- IRS instructions: https://www.irs.gov/instructions/i1120h

#### Arizona State — Form 120A or 120

Critical point: **Arizona does NOT follow federal IRC § 528** for tax exemption purposes. Arizona Revised Statutes § 43-1201(A) states HOAs qualifying under IRC § 528 are **not exempt from Arizona income tax**.

- HOAs must file an **Arizona corporate income tax return on Form 120A or Form 120**
- Exception: HOAs organized as 501(c)(4) or 501(c)(7) nonprofits are exempt under Arizona law and file **Arizona Form 99** instead
- **Arizona also requires** an annual financial audit, review, or compilation (see Section 8 above)

Source: https://hoatax.com/arizona-hoa-tax-return-filing-requirements/

#### Practical guidance for a tiny self-managed HOA

A small self-managed HOA like Goswick Ranch HOA should:
1. File IRS Form 1120-H annually (even if no tax owed — to claim Section 528 election)
2. File Arizona Form 120A or 120 annually (even if no state tax owed)
3. Consult a CPA with HOA experience for at least the first year of setup; filings are relatively straightforward thereafter
4. Consider organizing as an Arizona nonprofit corporation to simplify tax status
