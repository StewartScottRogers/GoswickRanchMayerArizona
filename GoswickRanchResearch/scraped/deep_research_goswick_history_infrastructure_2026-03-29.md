## TRACK B: WATER RIGHTS AND RURAL INFRASTRUCTURE

### 6. Prescott Active Management Area — Is Mayer Inside or Outside?

**Key finding:** Mayer and the Goswick Ranch area appear to be **outside the Prescott AMA**, though they are near its boundary.

**What the Prescott AMA covers:** The Prescott AMA encompasses the **Little Chino Sub-basin** and the **Upper Agua Fria Sub-basin**, including: Prescott, Prescott Valley, Chino Valley, part of Dewey-Humboldt, and some unincorporated Yavapai County areas. It does **not** include Williamson Valley, Paulden, or Big Chino Valley.

**Mayer's position:** Mayer sits in the **lower Agua Fria Valley** downstream of Dewey-Humboldt. Multiple sources reference the "Upper Agua Fria Watershed Partnership" with a chair based in Mayer, indicating Mayer is treated as a **rural watershed management area, not an AMA**. The Prescott AMA boundary is centered on the upper valley (Prescott/Prescott Valley elevation ~5,000 ft); Mayer is further southeast.

**Practical implication for Goswick Ranch residents:**
- **Outside AMA:** No Assured Water Supply (AWS) requirement for individual well owners. No groundwater pumping permits required for domestic wells pumping ≤35 gpm (exempt wells under ARS 45-454).
- Must file **Notice of Intention to Drill (NOI)** with ADWR before drilling any well.
- Well must meet ADWR minimum construction standards and be drilled by a licensed contractor.
- No water use monitoring or reporting required for exempt domestic wells.
- The state's **Adequate Water Supply** disclosure rules still apply to subdivisions (developers must show 100-year supply before subdividing into >5 lots), but individual owners are not regulated.

### 7. Well Water in the Goswick Ranch Area

**Geology:** The Goswick Ranch sits in the **Big Bug Mining District** of the Bradshaw Mountains. Bedrock is Bradshaw granite intruded by diorite, granodiorite, and Precambrian schist. All wells tap **fractured bedrock aquifers** — there is no large sedimentary aquifer here as in the valley floors.

**Well characteristics in fractured granite:**
- Water is found in fracture zones; depth to productive fractures varies widely
- Typical productive zones in granite: 40–160 meters (130–525 ft) below surface
- Individual lots in Goswick Ranch already have some **private wells drilled** (mentioned in listings: "private well already on property")
- No community water system; each parcel is self-sufficient

**Water quality — ARSENIC WARNING:**
This is a significant finding for Goswick Ranch residents and prospective buyers.

- **43.6% of groundwater samples in Yavapai County exceed the EPA's 10 ppb arsenic limit** (NCBI study, 2020, covering 1990–2017 data)
- The Mayer Domestic Water Improvement District (which serves Mayer town center and Poland Junction via 19 wells) reports arsenic at **4.0 ppb** in its treated system water — this is below the legal limit but **1,000x above EWG's health guideline of 0.004 ppb**
- The district also detects: total trihalomethanes (137x EWG guideline), bromoform (34x), dibromochloromethane (31x), haloacetic acids (24x), nitrate (24x). All are within federal legal limits but above health-based thresholds.
- The **Lynx Creek/Walker Mining District** (a few miles west toward Prescott) has documented arsenic contamination in private wells from acid mine drainage. Poland Junction was specifically mentioned in ADEQ complaint documentation (2012 report context).
- **Big Bug Mining District** (which directly encompasses the Goswick Ranch area) is the Au-Ag-Cu-Pb-Zn mining area on the northeast slopes of the Bradshaws. Past mining activity increases risk of metal leaching into groundwater.

**Recommendation for residents:** Private well owners in Goswick Ranch should **test their well water every 5 years** (per ADHS/UA Extension recommendation) and specifically test for arsenic, lead, nitrates, and mining-related metals. Free testing has been available through ADHS/UA Cooperative Extension grants (check current availability at [azdhs.gov/wellwater](https://www.azdhs.gov/wellwater)).

**ADWR Well Registry:** To research specific wells on or near Goswick Ranch parcels, use the interactive map at [azwatermaps.azwater.gov/wellreg](https://azwatermaps.azwater.gov/wellreg/) or search by parcel number at [app.azwater.gov/WellRegistry](https://app.azwater.gov/WellRegistry/SearchWellReg.aspx).

### 8. Rainwater Harvesting in Arizona

**Legal status:** Rainwater harvesting is **legal, encouraged, and supported** by Arizona state law.

**Key rules (as of 2025):**
- **No tank size limit** imposed by state law for residential systems
- **No permit required** for outdoor irrigation use or non-potable indoor use (toilet flushing)
- **Active systems storing >10,000 gallons** or used for purposes other than domestic use require an ADWR permit
- Indoor **potable** use allowed if system meets filtration and disinfection requirements and local building codes
- Water must be used on the property where it is collected

**Grant program (2024–2025 ADWR Rainwater Harvesting Program):**
- FY2024–2025: $1,000,000 appropriated from state general fund
- **Passive systems:** Up to 50% reimbursement, maximum **$500**
- **Active systems:** Based on tank capacity:
  - 50–799 gallon tank: $0.25/gallon reimbursement
  - 800+ gallon tank: **$1.00/gallon**, maximum **$2,000**
- Must submit project plan for ADWR approval before installation; reimbursement based on submission date

**Tax credits:** No specific Arizona state income tax credit for rainwater harvesting (separate from solar). The grants above are the financial incentive mechanism.

**Practical note for 9-acre rural Goswick Ranch property at 6,000 ft:** At approximately 14–16 inches annual precipitation (Bradshaw Mountains foothills), a 2,500 sq ft roof catchment area could yield ~15,000–20,000 gallons annually. Useful for livestock watering, irrigation, and emergency water storage.

### 9. Private Road Law in Arizona

**Is Goswick Ranch Rd a private or county road?**
- Multiple real estate listings confirm the internal subdivision roads (S Goswick Ranch Rd, S Hawk Mountain Trail, S Miners Pick Rd, E Vaca Bonita Trail, E Elizabeth Mine Rd, Bullhorn Rd, E Pearl Mountain Rd) are **private, gated roads** maintained by the HOA.
- One Mayer-area listing noted "county maintained road up to Pack Train Trail, then privately maintained" — consistent with Goswick Ranch roads being private once past the gate.
- Yavapai County Public Works confirms: when a street name is officially recognized, the county may assign addresses along it, but **the maintenance, signage responsibility, and liability remain with the private owners**.
- Yavapai County Public Works contact: (928) 771-3183 or web.public.works@yavapai.us

**Arizona statutory framework:**
- **ARS Title 33, Chapter 16** (Arizona Planned Communities Act) governs HOAs and planned communities
- **ARS 33-1818:** The HOA association is responsible for maintenance of **private roadways** if this obligation is specified in the CC&Rs. Associations may also enter agreements with local government for maintenance assistance.
- If no road maintenance agreement exists among property owners, costs are divided proportionately by usage.
- ARS 33-1818 also addresses HOA authority over public roadways (grandfathered communities that regulate public streets must hold a membership vote by June 30, 2025 to retain that authority).

**Annual gravel road maintenance costs:**
- Estimates vary widely: $1,380 to $5,452 per mile for basic gravel road maintenance
- A 2005 World Bank study found ~$2,371/mile/year (inflation-adjusted)
- Arizona-specific costs are not well-documented publicly; contact Yavapai County Public Works or a local grading contractor for current rates
- For Goswick Ranch (estimated 2–3 miles of internal roads serving ~50+ lots): rough estimate $5,000–$15,000/year in gravel road maintenance; the $216/year HOA dues likely covers basic gate/road maintenance plus reserves

### 10. Solar Energy and APS Net Metering

**APS service territory:** APS (Arizona Public Service) is confirmed as the **electric utility serving Mayer and Poland Junction**. (APS customers in Mayer/Poland Junction reported power loss during the 2017 Goodwin Fire evacuation.)

**APS net billing (not true net metering — APS moved to net billing in 2017):**
- New solar customers are credited at a **buy-back (export) rate**, not the full retail rate
- Current rate (through August 31, 2025): **$0.0685/kWh** (6.85 cents)
- Rate from September 1, 2025: **$0.0617/kWh** (6.17 cents)
- Rate is **locked for 10 years** from interconnection date
- APS reduces the available rate by up to **10% each September**
- Rate history: started at $0.129/kWh in 2017; has declined ~52% over 8 years
- Comparison: APS retail rate ~$0.15/kWh; peak summer rates can exceed $0.25/kWh; the buy-back rate is approximately **40–41% of retail**
- Alternative: EPR-2 (variable credits based on on-peak/off-peak rates)
- **Battery storage** is increasingly attractive given the low export rate — store excess solar for self-consumption at higher retail rate value

**Solar incentives summary (as of March 2026):**

| Incentive | Amount | Status |
|---|---|---|
| Federal ITC (30%) | ELIMINATED | Ended Jan 1, 2026 (signed July 4, 2025) |
| AZ State Solar Tax Credit | 25% of cost, max **$1,000** | Still active (no end date) |
| AZ Property Tax Exemption | Solar added value not taxed | Still active |
| AZ Sales Tax Exemption | 5.6% saved on equipment | Still active |
| APS export rate (10-yr lock) | $0.0617/kWh (Sept 2025+) | Active |

**Solar potential at 6,000 ft, central AZ:**
- Arizona averages 5.5–6.5 peak sun hours/day
- At 6,000 ft elevation: ~12.5% more irradiance per 1,000m of elevation gain → roughly **6.5–7.5 peak sun hours/day** for Goswick Ranch area
- Clear skies 300+ days/year at this latitude
- **One of the best solar locations in the country** despite elimination of federal tax credit
