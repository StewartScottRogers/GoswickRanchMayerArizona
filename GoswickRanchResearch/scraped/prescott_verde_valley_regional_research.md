## TOPIC A: Prescott Culture and Practical Services

### 1. Prescott Overview

**Source:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prescott,_Arizona

Prescott (elevation 5,319 ft; 2020 population 45,827) is the county seat of Yavapai County, established in 1864. It served as Arizona Territory's capital three separate times before Phoenix took the role permanently in 1889. The city has a celebrated "wild west" history — Doc Holliday and Virgil Earp both passed through before their Tombstone era. Today Prescott is known for its walkable historic downtown, Victorian architecture, a strong arts scene, and a mild four-season mountain climate.

### 2. Whiskey Row

**Sources:** https://www.prescott.com/whiskey-row | https://www.prescott.com/whiskey-row-restaurants-bars | https://foundingfatherscollective.com/best-bars-in-prescott/

Whiskey Row is the block of Montezuma Street flanking Courthouse Plaza, famous for housing dozens of saloons in the 19th-century frontier period (reportedly 40 saloons at peak). The block burned in 1900; legend holds that drinkers carried the Palace bar out to the plaza to continue drinking during the fire.

**Currently operating establishments include:**

- **The Palace Restaurant & Saloon** — Arizona's oldest saloon and the state's oldest continuous business; rebuilt after the 1900 fire; known for steaks and historic memorabilia
- **Matt's Saloon** — Country dancing, live bands, classic Prescott nightlife
- **Jersey Lilly Saloon** — Known for its second-story balcony, laid-back atmosphere
- **Bird Cage Saloon** — Casual, eclectic decor
- **Whiskey River Tavern** — Voted Best Bar on Whiskey Row for three consecutive years
- **Lonesome Valley Brewing Whiskey Row Taproom** — Small-batch craft beers
- **Prescott Brewing Company** — Family-friendly brewpub directly across from Courthouse Square, opened March 1994; award-winning beers. Address: Gurley Street.
- **The Point Bar & Lounge** — Upscale craft cocktails, speakeasy vibe; signature smoked old fashioned

The vibe today is a mix of historic Western saloon character alongside modern gastropubs and craft cocktail bars. The area attracts both locals and tourists and is walkable from the Courthouse Plaza.

### 4. Sharlot Hall Museum

**Sources:** https://sharlothallmuseum.org | https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sharlot_Hall_Museum

One of Arizona's premier history museums, on nearly four acres of grounds in downtown Prescott.

- **Buildings:** 11 exhibit buildings total; 6 are historic structures. Key buildings include:
  - Governor's Mansion (1864, built on-site; on the National Register of Historic Places)
  - Fort Misery — oldest log cabin in Arizona (1864), moved to museum grounds in 1934
  - Frémont House (1875) — home of 5th Territorial Governor John C. Frémont
  - Bashford House (1877)
- **Hours:** May–September: Mon–Sat 10 AM–5 PM, Sun noon–4 PM. October–April: Mon–Sat 10 AM–4 PM, Sun noon–4 PM.
- **Admission:** Adults (18–64) $9 | Seniors (65+) $8 | Active military $6 | Youth (13–17) $5 | Children 12 and under free
- **Library & Archives:** Full-service research facility across the street; rare books, original documents, photographs, maps, oral histories.

### 5. Phippen Museum of Western Art

**Sources:** https://phippenartmuseum.org | https://www.prescott.com/phippen-museum

### 13. Verde Valley Wine Trail

**Sources:** https://vvwinetrail.com | https://verdecanyonrr.com | https://verdevalleywine.org | https://wherearethosemorgans.com/best-sedona-wineries-verde-valley-wine-trail/

**Key Wineries:**

| Winery | Location | Notes |
|--------|----------|-------|
| **Alcantara Vineyards** | Verde River / Oak Creek confluence, near Cottonwood | One of the largest central AZ vineyards; live music, events; hay rides, horseback, e-bike, Jeep tours, kayaking, tubing |

### 14. Jerome, Arizona

**Source:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerome,_Arizona | https://jeromegrandhotel.net | https://jeromeghosttours.com

**Quick facts:**
- Population: 464 (2020 census)
- Elevation: 4,600 ft
- Location: Yavapai County, Cleopatra Hill; ~40 miles from Mayer (~50 minutes drive)
- **National Historic Landmark** (designated 1967)

**History:** Jerome was founded in the late 19th century as a copper mining boom town; at its peak in the 1920s, 10,000+ people lived here. The United Verde Mine and United Verde Extension (UVX) Mine were among the richest copper deposits ever found in North America. Mining ceased in 1953; the town's population fell below 100. Artists, hippies, and entrepreneurs began reviving the town in the 1970s; today it is a thriving art-and-tourism destination.

**Current Attractions:**
- **Jerome State Historic Park** — Housed in the Douglas Mansion (1916 adobe home of mine owner James S. Douglas); overlooking the open pit mine; mining history exhibits
- **Mine Museum** — Operated by the Jerome Historical Society
- **Caduceus Cellars Tasting Room** — Maynard Keenan's winery in a historic building in town
- **Art galleries, coffee houses, boutique shops** — The "Main Street" of Jerome winds up the hillside with galleries and shops in old Victorian buildings; notable for fine art, antiques, and craft shops
- **The Sliding Jail** — Jerome's famous concrete jail that slowly moved downhill due to mine subsidence (moved ~225 feet over the decades)

**Jerome Grand Hotel:**
- Originally the United Verde Hospital (1927) — built by the Phelps Dodge Mining Company; one of the most modern medical facilities in the American West at the time
- During its 30 years as a hospital, nearly 9,000 patients died within its walls
- Closed 1950; stood empty for 44 years
- Reopened as the **Jerome Grand Hotel in 1996** after heavy renovation
- Currently operating; features the **Asylum Restaurant** (lunch and dinner daily except Tuesday); 24/7 staffed lobby; standard and specialty rooms
- **Ghost hunting tours:** Available Monday–Thursday; the hotel is considered one of Arizona's most famously haunted locations. Ghost hunters and private tours are restricted from guest floors.

**Ghost Tours:** Multiple operators offer Jerome ghost tours (Jerome Ghost Tours at jeromeghosttours.com; US Ghost Adventures). Jerome's haunted reputation is a major draw.

**Hours:** Spring 6 AM–7 PM | Summer 6 AM–8 PM | Fall/Winter 7 AM–6 PM

### 16. Tuzigoot National Monument

**Sources:** https://www.nps.gov/tuzi | https://www.visitarizona.com/places/parks-monuments/tuzigoot-national-monument

**Location:** Near Clarkdale, AZ (Verde Valley); mailing address Camp Verde, AZ; Phone: (928) 634-5564
**Distance from Mayer:** ~27 miles northeast (approximately 35–40 minutes)

**What it is:** A remarkably preserved Sinagua pueblo of approximately 110 rooms built on a hilltop ridge above the Verde Valley and the Tavasci Marsh. The pueblo was occupied from approximately AD 1000 to 1425. The hilltop setting provides sweeping views of the Verde Valley.

**What to see:**
- The hilltop pueblo itself (exterior viewing; visitors walk the perimeter)
- Museum with artifacts from the site
- Tavasci Marsh wetlands — native wildlife and vegetation below the hill
- Dark sky viewing opportunities

**Hours:** Daily 8 AM–4 PM, year-round; closed Christmas Day

**Admission:** $10 per adult (16+); children 15 and under free. **This ticket is also valid for admission to Montezuma Castle National Monument** within the same 7-day period. America the Beautiful passes accepted.

### 17. Montezuma Castle National Monument

**Sources:** https://www.nps.gov/moca | https://www.visitarizona.com/like-a-local/your-guide-to-exploring-montezuma-castle-national-monument

**Location:** Camp Verde, AZ; Phone: (928) 567-3322
**Distance from Mayer:** ~28 miles east (approximately 35–40 minutes)

**What it is:** One of the best-preserved cliff dwellings in North America. The "castle" is a 5-story, 20-room dwelling built into a natural limestone alcove in a towering cliff face, constructed by the Sinagua people around AD 1100–1350. It was never actually a castle nor connected to Montezuma — both names are misnomers given by 19th-century settlers. The monument also includes **Montezuma Well**, a natural limestone sink (sinkhole) 11 miles north, fed by 1.5 million gallons of spring water daily, with additional Sinagua irrigation canals and cliff dwellings.

**Visitor experience:** Viewing is from a paved 1/3-mile loop trail below the cliff (the dwelling itself is not accessible to visitors, preserving it from foot traffic). The site is visually dramatic and educational.

**Hours:** Daily 8 AM–5 PM; Montezuma Well picnic area 8 AM–4 PM; closed Christmas Day and New Year's Day; closes at 2 PM on Thanksgiving and Christmas Eve.

**Admission:** $10 per adult (16+); children 15 and under free. **Same ticket valid at Tuzigoot National Monument** for 7 days. Interagency/America the Beautiful passes accepted.

**Day trip pairing:** Tuzigoot and Montezuma Castle are ~23.5 miles apart (about 40 minutes via SR 260), making them ideal to combine in a single day trip from Goswick Ranch, along with Dead Horse Ranch and a stop in Cottonwood wine country — all within roughly 30–50 miles of Mayer.

### 18. Sedona, Arizona

**Sources:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sedona,_Arizona | https://visitsedona.com | https://www.sedona.net/parking-in-sedona | https://vanlifebydesign.com

**Quick facts:**
- Population: 9,684 (2020)
- Located in both Coconino and Yavapai counties, within the Coconino National Forest
- Distance from Mayer: **~60 miles, approximately 1 hour drive**

**Character:** Sedona is defined by its spectacular red rock formations — towering sandstone buttes and mesas that glow orange, pink, and red at sunrise and sunset. The landscape supports hundreds of hiking and mountain biking trails. The city is also known as a New Age and spiritual tourism destination; practitioners claim "energy vortexes" at Bell Rock, Airport Mesa, Cathedral Rock, and Boynton Canyon (no scientific basis, but culturally significant to many visitors). The Chapel of the Holy Cross (1956), built into a redrock cliff face, is a major non-denominational landmark.

**Best access route from Mayer:**
- **Primary route:** SR 69 south → I-17 north to Exit 298 → SR 179 north into Sedona (~60 miles, ~1 hour). SR 179 is a federally designated All-American Road for its scenic quality.
- **Scenic alternative:** I-17 to SR 260 west → Cottonwood → SR 89A east through Clarkdale and Oak Creek Canyon into West Sedona. This route passes wineries and Jerome and is excellent for a full Verde Valley day trip.
- **Oak Creek Canyon (SR 89A):** A famously scenic 12-mile stretch between Sedona and Flagstaff through a dramatic red-rock canyon with swimming holes (Slide Rock State Park); accessed from Sedona northbound.

**Top accessible sights:**
- **Cathedral Rock** — Among Arizona's most photographed landmarks; trailhead accessible via SR 179 and Back O Beyond Road; moderate 1.5-mile trail to a saddle with dramatic views
- **Bell Rock** — Prominent red mesa along SR 179 at Village of Oak Creek; easily visible from the road; easy to moderate hiking
- **Chapel of the Holy Cross** — Off SR 179; free, open daily; dramatic architecture built into a cliff
- **Airport Mesa** — Short drive up Airport Road; panoramic 360-degree views

**Parking and crowds — important for residents:**
Sedona receives millions of visitors annually and parking is the single biggest frustration at peak times. Key notes:
- Popular trailheads (Devil's Bridge, Cathedral Rock, Soldier Pass) can reach capacity by **7:00 AM** on busy weekends
- A **Red Rock Pass** ($5/day) is required to park at most Forest Service trailheads; America the Beautiful passes also accepted
- **Free shuttle system** operates at busy trailheads during peak hours; when shuttles run, Cathedral Rock and Soldier Pass trailhead parking lots are closed to private vehicles
- **Best strategy for Goswick Ranch day-trippers:** Go on a weekday, arrive early (before 8 AM for popular hikes), or use the shuttle system

**Airport note:** Sedona Airport is general aviation only (no commercial flights).

### 20. Fort Verde State Historic Park

**Sources:** https://azstateparks.com/fort-verde | https://visitcampverde.com/fort-verde-state-historic-park/

**Location:** 125 E Hollamon St, Camp Verde, AZ 86322; (928) 567-0066
**Distance from Mayer:** ~35 miles via I-17 N (~40 minutes)

Commemorates the U.S. Army post that was General George Crook's base of operations during the Apache Wars (1865–1891). The preserved 1870s–1880s officers' quarters are among the most intact examples of frontier military architecture in Arizona.

- **What to see:** Three historic house museums with period furnishings (listed on National and State Registers of Historic Places); Visitor Center with interpretive exhibits and military artifacts; history of the Verde Valley campaigns and Indian Wars
- **Living history programs:** Periodically scheduled; check ahead
- **Hours:** Daily 10 AM–4 PM; Thanksgiving 10 AM–2 PM; Christmas Eve 10 AM–2 PM; closed Christmas Day
- **Admission:** Adults (14+) $7 | Youth (7–13) $4 | Children (0–6) free
- **Amenities:** Picnic tables, restrooms, RV/tour bus parking, ADA accessible

### 21. Yavapai County General Plan / Comprehensive Plan

**Sources:** https://www.yavapaiaz.gov/Development-and-Permits/Development-Services/Planning-Unit/Comprehensive-Plan | https://yplan.org/sr69/ | https://planningyavapai2032.org

**Current Plan:** The Yavapai County Comprehensive Plan 2032 was officially adopted by the Board of Supervisors on March 15, 2023. A January 2026 update version is also on file (PDF available at yavapaiaz.gov, but access was blocked during this research session).

**Core goals of the Comprehensive Plan:**
- Supports Communities through Collaboration
- Ensures Healthy Communities and Quality of Life
- Preserves and Embraces Rural Heritage
- Encourages Responsible and Balanced Growth
- Responsible Stewardship of Natural Resources
- Promotes Economic Development

### 23. Arcosanti

**Source:** https://www.arcosanti.org | roadsideamerica.com

**Location:** Off I-17 Exit 262 (Arcosanti Road), approximately 1.5 miles (2.5 km) northeast of the highway on an unpaved road; near Cordes Junction, AZ
**Distance from Goswick Ranch:** Approximately 11–15 miles (roughly 15–20 minutes) — an extremely short drive making it one of the closest cultural attractions to the Ranch

**What it is:** Arcosanti is a visionary architectural experiment founded in 1970 by Italian-American architect **Paolo Soleri** (1919–2013). Soleri coined the term "arcology" — the fusion of architecture and ecology — to describe his concept of dense, human-scale urban environments that minimize sprawl and resource consumption. Arcosanti was conceived as a working prototype of this philosophy, built incrementally by architecture students, volunteers, and researchers over decades on a mesa above the Agua Fria River.

The structures are striking brutalist-influenced concrete forms, including large apsidal vaults and amphitheaters. The campus is never "finished" — construction has continued for over 50 years and remains ongoing.

**Current status:**
- **Visitor Center / Gift Shop:** Open Thursday–Monday, 9 AM–5 PM; closed Tuesday–Wednesday. Also closed New Year's Day, Thanksgiving, Christmas.
- **Guided tours:** Thursday–Monday at 9:30 AM, 11:00 AM, and 2:00 PM; reservations strongly recommended as they fill quickly. Day visitors must take a guided tour to explore the grounds.
- **Cafe:** Currently CLOSED
- **What tours include:** The Arcosanti grounds, architecture, and live demonstrations in the **bronze foundry** and **ceramics workshop** (weekdays)
- **Overnight accommodations:** Guest rooms available
- **Hiking trails** on the mesa
- **Gift shop:** Features handmade Cosanti windbells — Soleri's famous cast bronze and ceramic wind bells, sold worldwide; the foundry at Arcosanti produces them on-site

**Why it matters for Goswick Ranch residents:** Arcosanti is one of the most unique architectural and philosophical landmarks in the American Southwest, visited by architects, students, artists, and travelers from around the world — yet it sits practically in Goswick Ranch's backyard. It makes for an easy afternoon excursion (tours run 90 minutes) that is unlike anything else in the region. The gift shop windbells make distinctive gifts and locally-made artisan souvenirs.

**Note:** The 1.5-mile access road from I-17 is unpaved but generally passable by standard vehicles.
