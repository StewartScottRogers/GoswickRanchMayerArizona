## Bradshaw Mountains

### Location & Dimensions
- **Location:** Central Arizona; approximately 5 miles south of Prescott
- **Orientation:** North–south range, ~40 miles long × ~25 miles wide
- **Eastern border:** Agua Fria River
- **Western border:** Hassayampa River
- **Part of:** Basin and Range region (also described as adjacent to Colorado Plateau)

### Elevation
| Peak | Elevation |
|------|-----------|
| Mount Union (highest) | 7,979 ft (2,432 m) |
| Mount Davis | 7,897 ft |
| Mount Tritle | 7,793 ft |
| Spruce Mountain | 7,696 ft |
| Towers Mountain | 7,628 ft |

The Goswick Ranch community sits in the foothills at approximately 4,400–6,000 ft elevation.

### Geology
- Primarily **Precambrian granite, gneiss, and schist** — ancient rock formations
- Highly mineralized: gold, silver, copper, lead, manganese, tungsten
- Over **$2 million** in gold extracted from Crown King Mine alone
- The Kwevkapaya Yavapai people mined copper here from approximately 1100–1600 AD

### Vegetation & Ecology
The biotic community ranges dramatically with elevation:
- **Lower elevations (Goswick Ranch area ~4,400–6,000 ft):** Interior chaparral, desert grassland, plains grassland
- **Mid elevations (6,000–7,000 ft):** Montane conifer forest — ponderosa pine dominant
- **High elevations (7,000–8,000 ft):** Mixed conifer: Douglas fir, white fir, quaking aspen

#### Wildlife
Mountain lions, black bears, mule deer, javelina, bobcats, wild turkey

### Human History
- **1863:** Gold discovered; placer mining began
- **Ghost towns:** Crown King, Bumble Bee, Cleator, Bradshaw City (40+ ghost towns in the range)
- **Mining era:** Late 1800s through early 1900s

### Protected Areas
- **Prescott National Forest** covers much of the range
- Bradshaw Ranger District encompasses the range and surrounds Prescott
- **Horsethief Basin** recreation area
- **Lynx Lake** recreation area — 55-acre lake; fishing, boating, hiking, mountain biking, birding
- Eagle Ridge Group Campground (ponderosa pine forest)

### Recreation (Prescott National Forest / Bradshaw District)
- ~950 miles of scenic trails total (forest-wide)
- ~450 miles of trails for hiking, backpacking, horseback riding, mountain biking
- OHV (off-highway vehicle) trails
- Equestrian camping: Groom Creek Horse Camp (7 mi south of Prescott, 6,400 ft)
- Year-round destination; mild climate
- **Ranger District contact:** Bradshaw Ranger District, Prescott National Forest
- **Forest website:** https://www.fs.usda.gov/r03/prescott

## Big Bug Creek

### Geography
- **Location:** Yavapai County, Arizona — in the Bradshaw Mountains
- **Coordinates:** Approx. 34.4364°N, -112.3648°W
- **Watershed HUC:** 15070102
- **Type:** Perennial to seasonal stream
- The creek begins just above the Breezy Pines community, flows through Mayer and Spring Valley

### Geology & Mining History
- Located in the **Big Bug Mining District** within the Bradshaw Mountains
- Placer gold deposits discovered in the **1860s**; greatest activity in the **1880s**
- Placers found in stream gravels and gravel-covered mesas in a ~20-mile triangular area northeast from the head of Big Bug Creek

### Water Quality
- USGS monitoring: Site USGS 09512500
- Water quality data: GLEC-MGBGB027.21

### Flood Risk (Critical Context)
The 2017 Goodwin Fire created hydrophobic soil conditions that dramatically increased flood risk. The creek itself is scenic and a key community feature, but **flood awareness is important for Goswick Ranch residents.**

## The 2017 Goodwin Fire and Big Bug Creek Flood

### The Fire
- **Detected:** June 24, 2017 (~14 miles south of Prescott in the Bradshaw Mountains)
- **Duration:** June 24 – July 10, 2017 (16 days)
- **Area burned:** 28,516 acres
- **Evacuations:** Mayer and Breezy Pines evacuated June 27; 9,000+ residents total

### The Flood (July 19, 2017)
- Fire scar created **hydrophobic (water-repellent) soil** — no natural absorption
- Rainfall: 1–2 inches in 30 minutes over sensitive drainages (4:15–4:45 PM)
- Big Bug Creek gauge (SR 69) peaked at **9.8 feet** at 5:51 PM = 7,291 cubic feet per second
- Multiple swift-water rescues performed

### Flood Damage
- **109 homes damaged** (13 major damage or total loss)
- Stagecoach mobile home park: "several homes lost"
- Spring Valley and Diamond Valley along Big Bug Creek also affected
- Evacuations for some areas lasted until August 19, 2017

### Source / Reference
- NWS Flagstaff flood analysis: https://www.weather.gov/fgz/MayerBigBugFlood2017
- Wikipedia Goodwin Fire: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goodwin_Fire

## Agua Fria National Monument (Nearby Recreation)

- **Managed by:** Bureau of Land Management (BLM)
- **Size:** 71,000 acres
- **Location:** ~40 miles north of central Phoenix; accessible via I-17 at exits 256 (Badger Springs), 259 (Bloody Basin), or Cordes Junction
- **Distance from Mayer:** Approximately 20–30 miles south/southeast via SR 69 and I-17
- **Admission:** Free; open 24/7 year-round
- **No drinking water on site** — bring your own
- **Cell service:** Unreliable

### Recreation Activities
- Hiking (no developed trails; popular: Badger Springs Trail)
- Wildlife viewing: coyotes, bobcats, deer, javelina
- Archaeological exploration (petroglyphs at Badger Springs/Agua Fria confluence; Pueblo la Plata)
- Off-highway vehicle use on designated roads
- Birding (designated Important Bird Area by Audubon Society)
- Dispersed camping (14-day limit)

### Safety Considerations
- Summer temps can exceed 110°F
- Flash flood risk in washes during monsoon season
- Rough, rocky terrain; steep canyons; no medical facilities

## Climate Summary (Geographic Context)

At Goswick Ranch's elevation (~4,400–6,000 ft):
- **Summer:** Warm and dry with afternoon monsoon thunderstorms (July–September)
- **Winter:** Cold with periodic snow; lows into the low 30s°F
- **Spring/Fall:** Mild and pleasant — the best outdoor seasons
- Growing season: ~235 days (late March through mid-November)
