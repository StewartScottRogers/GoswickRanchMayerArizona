## Background Context: Why This Matters for Goswick Ranch

Goswick Ranch sits approximately 1 mile east of AZ Route 69 near Mayer, AZ 86333, in the Bradshaw Mountains foothills of Yavapai County. The community faces compounding risks:

- **Wildfire:** The Bradshaw Ranger District of Prescott National Forest borders the area. The 2017 Goodwin Fire burned 28,516 acres nearby. The April 2022 Crooks Fire burned 9,402 acres near Mt. Union (~10 miles south of Prescott). The Brady Fire (February 2025) burned 400 acres near Pine Flats, just west of Mayer, triggering GO evacuations. APS has identified Mayer as a community subject to Public Safety Power Shutoffs during extreme fire danger.
- **Post-fire flooding:** The Goodwin Fire scar caused catastrophic flooding of Big Bug Creek on July 19, 2017, damaging 109 homes in under 4 hours. This is the defining local disaster event.
- **WUI classification:** The community is firmly in the Wildland-Urban Interface, which affects insurance availability, fire response, and property hardening requirements.

## 1. Wildfire Preparedness

### 1.1 Arizona Defensible Space Requirements

Arizona follows a three-zone model. Local ordinances may be stricter than state minimums; Mayer Fire District can advise on local requirements.

**Zone 0 — Immediate Zone (0–5 feet from structure):**
Not yet legally required statewide but strongly recommended by DFFM. Guidelines include using non-combustible materials (gravel, pavers, concrete) instead of wood mulch; removing dead weeds, dry grass, and debris; eliminating combustible items stored against the house.

**Zone 1 — Intermediate Zone (5–30 feet):**
- Outbuildings and LPG/propane tanks need 10 feet of cleared space to bare soil, plus an additional 10-foot perimeter of cleared vegetation
- Remove low-hanging limbs (up to 6 feet from ground on trees)
- Vertical spacing: shrub height × 3 = required clearance to lowest tree branch (e.g., a 5-foot shrub needs 15 feet to lowest branch above it)
- Remove dead plants, grass, and debris
- Keep grasses mowed short

**Zone 2 — Extended Zone (30–100 feet, or to property line):**
- Space trees so crowns have 18+ feet between them
- Limb trees 6–10 feet from ground
- Remove dead vegetation and branches
- Interrupt fire's path to the home by removing ladder fuels

**Arizona-Specific Guidance:**
- Source: [Arizona Fire Marshals Association — Creating Defensible Spaces](https://www.azfiremarshals.org/creating-defensible-spaces)

### 1.3 Fire Restriction Levels — How They Work in Arizona

Arizona uses a Stage system managed by the Arizona Department of Forestry and Fire Management (DFFM) on state and private lands, with separate orders from the U.S. Forest Service on federal lands.

**Stage 1:** Campfires allowed only in developed sites with fire rings; restrictions on open burning, charcoal use in high-risk areas; no smoking while traveling through wildland areas.

**Stage 2:** No campfires anywhere; stricter prohibitions including fireworks, target shooting with tracer/incendiary ammunition, and chainsaws without spark arrestors.

**How Yavapai County announces restrictions:**

## 2. Evacuation Planning

### 2.1 The Ready, Set, Go! Program

All 15 Arizona county sheriffs — including Yavapai — have formally adopted the Ready, Set, Go! (RSG) program. The three levels correspond to escalating danger:

**READY (ongoing):**
Always be prepared. Maintain defensible space. Build and maintain a go kit. Make a family evacuation and communication plan. Know your evacuation zone. Sign up for alerts.

**SET (threat approaching):**
Significant danger is present. Consider voluntarily evacuating early — especially for those with pets, livestock, mobility limitations, or who are dependent on medical equipment. Gather your go kit, load vehicles, confirm family communications plan. Residents who leave early are safer and free emergency routes for those with less mobility.

**GO (imminent danger):**
Evacuate immediately. Follow designated evacuation routes. Do not wait for official orders if you feel unsafe. Do not return until officials declare it safe. Call 911 if you need evacuation assistance.

- Arizona EIN page: [https://ein.az.gov/ready-set-go](https://ein.az.gov/ready-set-go)

### 2.2 Yavapai County Evacuation Zones

Yavapai County uses a zone-based evacuation system. Zones are labeled with a combination of letters (representing the geographic area) and numbers (subdividing into smaller zones). During an emergency, specific zones are placed on SET or GO status rather than broad geographic areas.

**The Brady Fire Example (Feb 2025):** Zones YCU-2174 and YCU-2216 covering Pine Flats were placed on GO status. This illustrates how zone-specific the system is — neighboring communities may not be affected while a specific zone evacuates.

**How to Find Your Zone:**
1. Visit [protect.genasys.com](https://protect.genasys.com) — type your address in the search bar
2. Download the free **Genasys Protect** app (App Store or Google Play)
3. The map shows your zone outlined in green; zone status is displayed below the zone ID
4. Learn more: [https://www.yavapaiready.gov/Resilience/Prepare/Know-Your-Zone](https://www.yavapaiready.gov/Resilience/Prepare/Know-Your-Zone)

Yavapai County OEM and YCSO launched Genasys Protect in April 2024. It replaced older systems and provides real-time zone status during emergencies.

**Evacuation Assistance:** Residents needing help evacuating (medical, mobility, no transportation) should call 911. Yavapai County also maintains functional-needs evacuation assistance; contact YCOEM at (928) 277-8032.

## 3. Flood Risk and Awareness

### 3.1 The 2017 Goodwin Fire / Big Bug Creek Flood: The Defining Event

This is essential context for every Goswick Ranch resident.

**The Fire:** June 24–July 10, 2017. Goodwin Fire burned 28,516 acres within the Big Bug Creek watershed in the Bradshaw Mountains. The fire created a **hydrophobic soil layer** — burned soils that repel water instead of absorbing it.

**The Flood — July 19, 2017:**
- 4:15–4:45 PM: 1–2 inches of rain fell in 30 minutes over the burned Goodwin Fire scar
- 5:45 PM: Flooding began in Mayer — water that couldn't soak into the hydrophobic soil drained directly into Big Bug Creek upstream
- 5:51 PM: Creek gauge hit 9.8 feet
- 6:45 PM: Flooding reached Spring Valley
- 8:00 PM: Flooding receded

**The Damage:** 109 homes damaged; 13 with major damage or total loss. Stagecoach mobile home park severely damaged with several homes destroyed. Multiple swift water rescues in Mayer.

**The Lesson:** A modest monsoon storm (1–2 inches in 30 minutes) that would normally be absorbed by healthy soil became a catastrophic flood because the burn scar eliminated infiltration. Recovery of soil hydrology takes 1–3 years.

- NWS Event Summary: [https://www.weather.gov/fgz/MayerBigBugFlood2017](https://www.weather.gov/fgz/MayerBigBugFlood2017)
- Wikipedia — Goodwin Fire: [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goodwin_Fire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goodwin_Fire)

### 3.2 Post-Fire Flood Risk — Ongoing Concern

Any significant fire burning in the Big Bug Creek watershed upstream of Mayer creates elevated flood risk during the subsequent monsoon season(s). Key points:

- **Burn scars increase flood risk for 1–3 years** after a fire. The hydrophobic layer persists until vegetation regrows and organic debris accumulates.
- **Debris flows** (not just water but mud, rocks, trees) are the most dangerous outcome and can travel at high speeds.
- **Monsoon rains are the trigger.** Arizona's monsoon (roughly June 15–September 30) brings intense but localized storms. Rainfall of 1 inch in 30 minutes over a burn scar is sufficient to generate dangerous flooding.
- **Watch Big Bug Creek gauge** — real-time data at Yavapai County Flood Control's ALERT system (updated every 5 minutes): [https://gis.yavapaiaz.gov/alert/](https://gis.yavapaiaz.gov/alert/)
- Specific gauge: Big Bug Creek at SR 69 (station YMBA3): [https://yavapaiaz.gov/ycflood/Weather/Weather-Stations-Gauges/ArtMID/6450/ArticleID/10997/3705-Big-Bug-Creek-at-SR69-YMBA3](https://yavapaiaz.gov/ycflood/Weather/Weather-Stations-Gauges/ArtMID/6450/ArticleID/10997/3705-Big-Bug-Creek-at-SR69-YMBA3)

- Yavapai County Flood After Fire page: [https://www.yavapaiaz.gov/Resident-Services/Flood-Control/Flood-Safety/Local-Flood-Hazard/Flood-After-Fire](https://www.yavapaiaz.gov/Resident-Services/Flood-Control/Flood-Safety/Local-Flood-Hazard/Flood-After-Fire)
- Arizona Water Resources — Flood After Fire: [https://www.azwater.gov/floodplain-management/flood-after-fire](https://www.azwater.gov/floodplain-management/flood-after-fire)

### 3.3 FEMA Flood Maps — Big Bug Creek

Big Bug Creek has an official FEMA flood designation:

- **Zone AE** — Special Flood Hazard Area with a 1% annual probability of flooding (the "100-year flood")
- Each project site in the area lies within the **regulatory floodway of Big Bug Creek**
- Applicable FIRM map numbers: **04025C2488H** and **04025C2470H** (effective date March 2, 2015)
- Look up any specific property: [https://msc.fema.gov/portal/search](https://msc.fema.gov/portal/search)
- FEMA Flood Maps overview: [https://www.fema.gov/flood-maps](https://www.fema.gov/flood-maps)

Properties within Zone AE are required by lenders to carry flood insurance if they have a federally backed mortgage.

### 3.4 Flood Insurance — National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP)

Standard homeowners insurance **does not cover flood damage.** Flood insurance must be purchased separately through the NFIP or a private insurer.

**Key facts for Mayer-area homeowners:**
- Flood insurance is available through the NFIP if your community participates (most municipalities do); check at [https://www.floodsmart.gov](https://www.floodsmart.gov)
- The standard 30-day waiting period for NFIP policies to take effect **may be waived** for properties affected by flooding caused or exacerbated by wildfire that started on adjacent federal land
- Even properties outside the mapped floodplain can flood — post-fire flooding can exceed the mapped floodplain
- FEMA's Yavapai County infrastructure scour protection grants demonstrate the county's known post-fire flood vulnerability

- Yavapai County Flood Insurance page: [https://www.yavapaiaz.gov/Resident-Services/Flood-Control/Flood-Protection-Insurance-and-Safety/Flood-Insurance](https://www.yavapaiaz.gov/Resident-Services/Flood-Control/Flood-Protection-Insurance-and-Safety/Flood-Insurance)

## 5. APS Public Safety Power Shutoffs (PSPS)

Arizona Public Service (APS) implemented a PSPS program in 2024, explicitly identifying Mayer as one of the high-fire-risk communities subject to shutoffs.

**How PSPS works:**
- APS monitors weather forecasts for extreme fire conditions (high winds, low humidity, dry fuels)
- When conditions trigger PSPS criteria, APS proactively cuts power to prevent downed lines from starting fires
- APS communicates via email, text, and phone calls in stages: planning notification (days in advance), daily timing updates, cancellation notices if conditions improve, restoration status updates

**Mayer is explicitly named** in the PSPS zone along with Potato Patch, Walker, Groom Creek, Wolf Creek, and others in Yavapai County.

**How to prepare for PSPS:**
- Ensure APS has your current email and phone (update at aps.com or call (800) 253-9405)
- Charge devices; maintain portable chargers
- Store flashlights, batteries, battery radio, non-perishable food, 1 gallon water/person/day
- Have backup supply of medications; plan for medical equipment dependent on power
- Learn manual garage door operation
- **Well-water users:** Well pumps require electricity. A minimum 3,000-watt generator is typically needed; factor in surge wattage (2–3× running watts at startup). Plan water storage if on a private well.

**Mayer Domestic Water Improvement District:** (928) 632-4113 | 12994 E. Central Ave | Office hours Mon–Fri 8AM–12PM. Check [mayerwaterdistrict.com](https://www.mayerwaterdistrict.com) for outage notices.

- APS PSPS program: [https://www.aps.com/psps](https://www.aps.com/psps)

### 8.2 HAM Radio — Yavapai County ARES/RACES

The **Amateur Radio Emergency Service (ARES) and Radio Amateur Civil Emergency Service (RACES)** for Yavapai County maintain emergency communication infrastructure that functions independently of commercial networks, cell towers, and internet.

**Mission:** Provide emergency communication support to agencies and communities during natural and man-made disasters.

**Weekly Nets (open to visitors):**
- Monday evenings, **1830 local time**: Mt. Union repeater (147.260 MHz, +0.600 offset, PL 103.5 Hz)
- Monday evenings, **1900 local time**: Mingus Mountain repeater (145.290 MHz, -0.600 offset, PL 127.3 Hz)
- Also active: Mt. Francis repeater (145.370 MHz, -0.600 offset, PL 127.3 Hz)
- National simplex calling frequency: 146.520 MHz
- Emergency simplex: 147.420 MHz

All three mountain-top repeaters have backup power systems and provide countywide coverage.

**2024 ARDC Grant:** ARES received funding to replace aging radios and antennas at their Mobile Amateur Radio Communications (MARC) van and two Emergency Operations Centers.

**How to join:** Licensed amateur radio operators can join. Basic training (FEMA IS courses) required.

- Website: [https://www.k7yca.org/](https://www.k7yca.org/)
- Frequencies/nets: [https://www.k7yca.org/on-the-air/frequencies/](https://www.k7yca.org/on-the-air/frequencies/)
- Yavapai Amateur Radio Club: [https://www.w7yrc.org/](https://www.w7yrc.org/)

**Why this matters for Goswick Ranch:** During a major fire or flood event, cell towers can fail or be overwhelmed. HAM radio nets provide reliable backup communication. A community member with a HAM license could serve as a liaison between the HOA and county emergency management.

## 9. Homeowners Insurance — Wildfire and Flood Risk

### 9.1 The Current Insurance Environment in Arizona

Arizona faces a growing homeowners insurance affordability and availability crisis driven by wildfire risk:

- Insurance rates increased **48% between 2021 and 2024** — third highest in the nation
- WUI properties in Yavapai County saw premiums surge another **12–18% in 2025**
- Wildfire deductibles jumped to **$5,000–$10,000** on many standard-market policies
- Non-renewal rates vary by county; some Arizona counties hit 4.8% in a single year
- **Arizona has no FAIR plan** (last-resort insurer) — unlike California, Colorado, or Florida. This is a critical gap.

**ISO Protection Class:** Much of Yavapai County carries ISO Class 9 or 10, indicating limited fire department water access and longer response times. This directly affects insurance rates and insurability.

### 9.2 Legislative Developments (2025)

**Arizona HB2800 (2025):** Provides a 60-day non-renewal pause after a declared wildfire emergency — insurers cannot cancel or refuse to renew within 60 days of a wildfire emergency declaration.

**Arizona HB2384:** Requires insurers to factor local Firewise designations into rates for municipalities under 150,000 population (Prescott qualifies; this may benefit communities in the Prescott-area fire district).

**Arizona Resiliency and Mitigation Council:** Formed December 2024, comprising DIFI, DFFM, fire services, insurance industry representatives. Released final report December 2025. Key finding: "There is no single all-encompassing solution — this issue must be addressed through a multifaceted approach."

- DIFI Homeowners Insurance page: [https://difi.az.gov/homeownersinsurance](https://difi.az.gov/homeownersinsurance)
- RMC Final Report 2025: [https://difi.az.gov/sites/default/files/2025-12/Resiliency%20and%20Mitigation%20Council%202025%20Final%20Report.pdf](https://difi.az.gov/sites/default/files/2025-12/Resiliency%20and%20Mitigation%20Council%202025%20Final%20Report.pdf)
- KJZZ — AZ insurance crisis overview: [https://www.kjzz.org/politics/2025-02-05/as-wildfire-risk-rises-across-arizona-insurance-companies-are-raising-rates-dropping-coverage](https://www.kjzz.org/politics/2025-02-05/as-wildfire-risk-rises-across-arizona-insurance-companies-are-raising-rates-dropping-coverage)
- UA Extension — Wildfire Insurance Non-Renewals AZ: [https://extension.arizona.edu/publication/wildfire-homeowners-insurance-non-renewals-arizona](https://extension.arizona.edu/publication/wildfire-homeowners-insurance-non-renewals-arizona)

### 9.3 What Homeowners Can Do to Maintain Coverage and Reduce Premiums

**Defensible space:**
- 100 feet of defensible space can yield a **10–20% reduction** on wildfire surcharges
- Document all mitigation work with photos and contractor invoices; submit to insurer at renewal
- Request re-inspection 60 days before renewal

**Firewise USA recognition:**
- USAA members in a recognized Firewise community receive up to **5% discount** on the total policy premium (Arizona eligible since 2/15/2016)
- Regional carriers have offered **5–15% credits** for recognized communities
- To date, USAA is the only major national carrier with an explicit Firewise discount; contact your insurer about potential credits
- NFPA — USAA discount page: [https://www.nfpa.org/education-and-research/wildfire/firewise-usa/become-a-firewise-usa-site/program-benefits/insurance-discounts-for-usaa-members-in-seven-states](https://www.nfpa.org/education-and-research/wildfire/firewise-usa/become-a-firewise-usa-site/program-benefits/insurance-discounts-for-usaa-members-in-seven-states)

**Home hardening:**
- Class A fire-rated roof (replaces wood shake, Class C)
- Non-combustible siding
- Ember-resistant vent covers (primary ember entry point during fires)
- Non-combustible fencing near the structure
- On-site water storage (2,500-gallon tank) — can improve ISO rating and insurability

**If dropped by your insurer:**
- File a complaint with Arizona DIFI: [https://difi.az.gov](https://difi.az.gov)
- Work with an independent agent who specializes in high-risk/WUI properties
- Arizona does not have a FAIR plan — persistence in the surplus lines market may be required
- Prescott-area reference: [Prescott Homeowners — Wildfire Insurance](https://inszoneinsurance.com/blog/prescott-homeowners-wildfire)

### 9.4 Flood Insurance

As noted in Section 3.4, standard homeowners insurance does not cover flood. If your property is in Zone AE (as many Big Bug Creek-adjacent parcels are), flood insurance may be required by your mortgage lender. Even properties outside Zone AE should consider coverage given post-fire flood risk.

## 10. Medical Emergencies

### 10.1 Mayer Fire District ALS Response

The Mayer Fire District provides Advanced Life Support (ALS) with paramedic capability confirmed at Station 23 (Cordes Lakes area). Station 22 (Spring Valley) is closest to Goswick Ranch. ALS capability means paramedics can administer medications and perform advanced interventions in the field — not just EMT-level stabilization.

**For life-threatening emergencies:** Call 911. The combined response from Mayer Fire and Native Air (see below) provides substantial capability for the rural setting.

### 10.2 Air Medical — Native Air Helicopters

**Native Air 4 (Prescott base):**
- Base: YRMC West Hospital, 1003 Willow Creek Road, Prescott, AZ
- Aircraft: Configured for high-altitude/mountainous terrain
- Coverage: Yavapai and Coconino Counties
- Capability: Trauma, cardiac, stroke, pediatric, burns, critical interfacility transport — 24/7/365

**First Responder Status (December 2024):**
Native Air 4 and the Prescott Valley base received "First Responder Status" in December 2024. This designation allows the helicopter pilot to identify a safe landing zone and the crew to begin patient care immediately upon landing — rather than waiting for ground crews to establish and secure a landing zone. This meaningfully improves response capability for rural scene calls.

**Additional coverage:** Native Air 14 and Native Air 83 (Cottonwood Airport) also respond to Yavapai County. Native Air 23 (Goodyear) supplements Yavapai County coverage.

- Source: [Prescott eNews — Native Air First Responder Status](https://prescottenews.com/2024/12/12/native-air-receives-first-responder-status-designation-helps-get-patients-to-hospitals-faster/)
- Native Air (Air Methods): [https://www.airmethods.com/air-medical/program/native-air-ambulance/](https://www.airmethods.com/air-medical/program/native-air-ambulance/)

### 10.3 Nearest Hospitals

**Yavapai Regional Medical Center (YRMC) — Prescott:**
- Two campuses: YRMC West (Prescott) and YRMC East (Prescott Valley)
- **Level IV Trauma Center** — provides initial trauma care, stabilization; can handle most emergencies but may transfer complex trauma to Phoenix
- YRMC West: 1003 Willow Creek Road, Prescott, AZ — approximately 27 miles, 36 minutes from Mayer
- YRMC Emergency Services: [https://www.yrmc.org/services/emergency-services/services](https://www.yrmc.org/services/emergency-services/services)

**Level I / Level II Trauma Centers (Phoenix):**
For the most severe trauma (major injuries, burns, complex surgeries), patients may be transported to Phoenix, approximately 73 miles / 1 hour 11 minutes from Mayer via I-17.

Phoenix Level I/II trauma centers include:
- Maricopa Medical Center (Level I — adults and pediatrics; only Level I for both in the state)
- HonorHealth Scottsdale Osborn (Level I)
- St. Joseph's Hospital (Level I)
- Abrazo West Campus (Level I)
- Valleywise Health (Level I pediatric trauma)

Arizona designated trauma centers map: [https://www.azdhs.gov/documents/preparedness/emergency-medical-services-trauma-system/trauma/traumacentersmap.pdf](https://www.azdhs.gov/documents/preparedness/emergency-medical-services-trauma-system/trauma/traumacentersmap.pdf)

**Practical implication:** For cardiac arrest, stroke, or major trauma at Goswick Ranch, the fastest pathway to definitive care may be Native Air helicopter to YRMC or direct to a Phoenix Level I center. Ground transport to YRMC (~36 minutes in good conditions) is the typical response for most ALS calls.

## 13. Gaps and Caveats

- **Mayer Fire response times:** No publicly available specific response time metrics. Contact the district directly at (928) 632-9534.
- **Goswick Ranch specific evacuation zone ID:** The exact Genasys zone designation for Goswick Ranch was not identified. Residents should look up their address at protect.genasys.com and record their zone ID.
- **Current Firewise communities near Mayer:** No confirmed list of Firewise-recognized communities in the immediate Mayer area was found. DFFM lists ~200 statewide; check the NFPA portal or contact Captain Matt Rust at DFFM.
- **Private road access:** If Goswick Ranch has private interior roads, their status during a PSPS or emergency may differ from public roads. The HOA should clarify road access agreements with Mayer Fire for large apparatus.
- **AlertYavapai vs. Smart911 URLs:** Two different registration URLs appear in different sources (Everbridge legacy and Smart911). Residents should confirm current enrollment by contacting YCSO at (928) 771-3260.
- **Yavapai CERT website** (yavapaicert.org) was unresponsive at time of research. Contact YCOEM for current CERT program status.
- **Insurance market changes:** The Arizona insurance market is rapidly evolving. Rates, availability, and legislation as of this research (March 2026) may change. Homeowners should consult an independent agent specializing in WUI properties.
